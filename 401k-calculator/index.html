Three things most workers miss with their 401(k)
1
Always capture the full employer match first. The employer match is the closest thing to free money in personal finance. If your company matches 100% up to 4% and you contribute only 2%, you are leaving thousands per year on the table. This is the highest-ROI move available to most workers, hands down.
2
Increase your contribution rate every time you get a raise. If you get a 3% raise and bump your contribution by 1%, you still take home more money than before. You barely notice the increase. Over a 30-year career, that habit alone can mean hundreds of thousands more at retirement.
3
Don't cash out when you leave a job. Cashing out a 401(k) before age 59 1/2 triggers federal income tax plus a 10% early withdrawal penalty. You can lose 30 to 40% of the balance overnight. Roll it into your new employer's 401(k) or into an IRA instead. Both options keep the money tax-deferred and growing.

What is a 401(k) and how does this 401k calculator work?

A 401(k) is an employer-sponsored retirement account that lets you contribute pre-tax dollars (Traditional) or after-tax dollars (Roth 401(k)) from each paycheck. Many employers match a percentage of your contributions, which is essentially free money added to your retirement savings.

This 401k calculator projects your future balance based on five core inputs: your salary, contribution percentage, employer match terms, current balance, and expected investment return. It also factors in salary growth over time and compounds returns monthly for accuracy. The year-by-year breakdown shows exactly how your money grows from contributions, employer match, and investment returns separately.

How much should you contribute to your 401(k)?

The minimum smart contribution is whatever percentage captures your full employer match. If your employer matches 100% up to 4% of salary, contributing 4% gets you an instant 100% return on that money. Anything less is leaving guaranteed money behind.

Beyond the match, a common target is 15% of gross income for retirement (combining your contributions and your employer's). If you started saving late, you may need 20% or more to catch up. Workers who maximize their 401(k) plus contribute to a Roth IRA on the side build wealth significantly faster than those who only do the match.

The 2026 IRS 401k max contribution is $23,500 for workers under age 50, with an additional $7,500 catch-up contribution allowed for workers 50 and older (total $31,000). Employer matching contributions are separate from this limit. You can verify current contribution limits directly at the IRS website.

Roth 401(k) vs Traditional 401(k): which one wins?

The Roth vs Traditional 401(k) decision comes down to one question: do you expect to be in a higher or lower tax bracket in retirement?

Traditional 401(k): Contributions are pre-tax. They reduce your taxable income today, so you pay less tax now. Withdrawals in retirement are taxed as ordinary income. Traditional wins if you expect a lower tax bracket in retirement (which is true for most retirees).

Roth 401(k): Contributions are post-tax. You pay full income tax now, but your withdrawals in retirement (including all the growth) come out tax-free. Roth wins if you expect a higher tax bracket in retirement, which is common for younger workers in lower brackets today who expect higher income later.

Understanding employer match: the free money rule

Employer matching is the single highest-return investment most workers will ever make. A 100% match means every dollar you contribute is matched dollar-for-dollar by your employer, up to a cap. That is an immediate 100% return on the matched portion of your contribution. No investment in the world beats that.

The most common match structures are: 100% match up to 3 to 6% of salary (full match), or 50% match up to 6 to 8% of salary (partial match). On a $65,000 salary with a 100% match up to 4%, your employer adds $2,600 per year to your account at no cost to you. Over 30 years at 7% return, that match alone grows to roughly $260,000.

If your contribution percentage is below your match cap, this calculator will warn you and show exactly how much money you are leaving on the table per year. Increasing your contribution to the cap is almost always the right financial move, with very few exceptions.

What is a good 401(k) balance by age?

Fidelity publishes commonly cited retirement savings benchmarks based on salary multiples by age:

  • By age 30: 1x annual salary
  • By age 40: 3x annual salary
  • By age 50: 6x annual salary
  • By age 60: 8x annual salary
  • By age 67: 10x annual salary

For a worker earning $75,000 per year, those benchmarks translate to $75K by 30, $225K by 40, $450K by 50, $600K by 60, and $750K by 67. These are rough guidelines, not requirements. Your actual target depends on retirement age, lifestyle, Social Security, and other income sources.

The 4% withdrawal rule explained

The 4% rule is a widely-used retirement income guideline: in your first year of retirement, you can withdraw 4% of your portfolio balance. In each following year, you adjust that dollar amount for inflation. The portfolio is designed to last about 30 years using this strategy with a balanced stock and bond mix.

A $1 million balance using the 4% rule gives you approximately $40,000 per year of pre-tax retirement income (about $3,333 per month). A $500K balance gives you about $20,000 per year. The annual income figure shown above your stats grid uses this 4% rule applied to your projected 401(k) balance.

The 4% rule has critics. Some financial educators argue 3.3 to 3.5% is safer for longer retirements. Others argue 5%+ is fine if you remain flexible. It is a starting point for thinking about retirement income, not a precise formula. Always consult a licensed financial advisor for personalized planning.

Common 401(k) mistakes to avoid

Not contributing enough for the full match. Already covered above. This is the most common and most expensive mistake.

Cashing out when changing jobs. A 401(k) cash-out before age 59 1/2 triggers federal income tax plus a 10% penalty. You can lose 30 to 40% of the balance instantly. Roll it into an IRA or your new employer's 401(k) instead.

Picking the default investment without checking fees. Many 401(k) plans default new participants into a target-date fund. These can be fine, but expense ratios vary widely. A fund charging 0.10% costs you about 1/10th of one charging 1.00% over a 30-year career. Check your plan's investment options for low-cost index funds.

Taking a 401(k) loan to fund lifestyle. Loans against your 401(k) feel cheap because you pay interest to yourself. But the borrowed money stops growing, you lose compound returns, and if you leave the job, the loan often becomes due immediately or converts to a taxable withdrawal. Use only for genuine emergencies, never to cover regular expenses.

Forgetting accounts at old jobs. The average American worker holds a dozen jobs in their career. Old 401(k) accounts get lost, plan administrators change, and statements stop arriving. Consolidate old accounts into a single IRA so you can manage and track them in one place. The U.S. Department of Labor has a free service to help find lost retirement accounts.

People Often Ask

How much should I put in my 401k?

At minimum, contribute enough to capture your full employer match. That is free money. Beyond that, a common target is 15% of gross income (including employer contributions). The 2026 contribution limit is $23,500 ($31,000 if age 50 or older).

What is the difference between a Roth 401(k) and a Traditional 401(k)?

A Traditional 401(k) reduces your taxable income now but withdrawals in retirement are taxed as ordinary income. A Roth 401(k) is funded with after-tax dollars but withdrawals in retirement are tax-free. Roth tends to win for younger workers who expect higher tax brackets later. Traditional tends to win for workers in high brackets now who expect lower retirement income.

What is a good 401k balance by age?

Fidelity's commonly cited benchmarks: 1x salary by age 30, 3x by 40, 6x by 50, 8x by 60, 10x by 67. These are rough guidelines, not requirements. Your actual target depends on retirement age, expected lifestyle, and other income sources like Social Security.

Can I contribute to both a 401(k) and a Roth IRA?

Yes, in most cases. A 401(k) and a Roth IRA have separate annual limits and are not affected by each other. For 2026, you can contribute up to $23,500 to a 401(k) plus up to $7,000 to a Roth IRA ($8,000 if 50 or older), subject to Roth IRA income limits. Use our Roth IRA Calculator to project the Roth side.

What is the 401k max contribution for 2026?

The 2026 employee 401(k) contribution limit is $23,500. Workers age 50 and older can make an additional $7,500 catch-up contribution, for a total of $31,000. Employer matching contributions are separate from this limit. Always verify current limits with the IRS.

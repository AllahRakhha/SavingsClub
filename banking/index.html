How to Choose a Bank Account

A plain-English guide to picking the right checking and savings accounts. What to look for, what to avoid, and how to make sure your money is safe.

Checking vs Savings: What's the Difference?

A checking account is built for movement. It's where your paycheck lands, where your rent leaves from, where your debit card pulls from. Banks expect you to use it constantly, so they make access easy: debit cards, paper checks, instant transfers, ATM withdrawals. The trade-off is that checking accounts pay little to no interest. Money sitting in checking is money not growing.

A savings account is built for storage. It's where you park money you don't need right now — your emergency fund, a vacation goal, a down payment in progress. Banks pay interest on savings because they can lend that money out. In exchange, federal rules limit how often you can withdraw from savings without triggering fees. Most people need both: checking for spending, savings for protecting the money they're not spending.

What to Look For in a Checking Account

Start with fees. The single biggest difference between checking accounts isn't features — it's whether the bank charges you to hold your own money. A "free" checking account should mean zero monthly maintenance fee with no minimum balance requirement. Some accounts advertise as free but charge $12 to $15 per month unless you maintain a balance of $1,500 or set up direct deposit. Read the actual terms, not the headline.

Next, check the ATM network. If your bank has 200 ATMs and the bank across town has 60,000, that gap will cost you. Out-of-network ATM fees typically run $3 to $5 per withdrawal, and your own bank may charge another $2 to $3 on top. That's $5 to $8 to access your own cash. Look for banks with large networks, ATM fee reimbursements, or both.

Direct deposit timing matters more than people realize. Some banks release your paycheck up to two days earlier than the official pay date. Over a year, getting paid 48 hours earlier doesn't change how much you make, but it reduces overdraft risk and gives you flexibility when bills land before payday. Other features worth checking: mobile check deposit, instant Zelle or peer-to-peer transfers, and whether the bank's mobile app is actually usable.

What to Look For in a Savings Account

APY is the number that matters most. APY stands for Annual Percentage Yield — it's the real rate of return on your money once you factor in compounding. The difference between a typical brick-and-mortar bank and a competitive online savings account is often 8 to 10 times. On $10,000 sitting in savings for a year, that's the difference between earning around $5 and earning around $400 to $450. The same money, the same FDIC protection, just a different bank.

Watch for teaser rates. Some banks advertise a high APY that only applies to balances under a certain threshold, or only for the first few months. The rate then drops to something unimpressive. Look for accounts with one flat APY that applies to your entire balance, no matter how much you have or how long you keep it there.

Minimum balance and fees come next. The best savings accounts have no minimum to open, no minimum to maintain, and no monthly fee. If a bank requires $1,000 to open a savings account, ask yourself why. Plenty of banks don't. Online Banks vs Traditional Banks

Online-only banks consistently offer higher APYs and lower fees than traditional banks. The reason is structural: no branches means no real estate costs, no lobby staff, no security guards, no utility bills for hundreds of locations. They pass some of that savings back to depositors in the form of better rates.

The trade-off is no in-person service. If you need to deposit cash regularly, prefer talking to a teller, or value walking into a branch when something goes wrong, an online-only bank may frustrate you. For most people who already manage their money through an app and rarely visit branches anyway, online banks offer significantly better value with no real downside.

A common setup that works well: keep a checking account at a traditional or hybrid bank for cash deposits and in-person needs, and keep a separate high-yield savings account at an online bank where your emergency fund and goal savings grow faster. The two accounts can be linked, and transfers usually take one to three business days.

Red Flags to Avoid

Any bank that isn't FDIC-insured is a red flag, full stop. The Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation protects deposits up to $250,000 per depositor, per bank. In the FDIC's 90-plus-year history, no depositor has lost a single penny of insured funds. If a bank or platform can't confirm FDIC membership or if the FDIC website doesn't list them — walk away. You can verify any U.S. bank at FDIC.gov in under a minute.

Watch for aggressive overdraft fee structures. Some banks charge $35 per overdraft and allow multiple overdrafts per day, meaning a single bad week can cost $100 or more. Better banks cap daily overdraft fees, give a grace period to bring your balance positive, or offer free overdraft transfers from savings. If you've ever been hit hard by overdraft fees, switching banks may be the highest-return financial move you make this year.

Be cautious of high minimum balance requirements that disguise themselves as "premium" accounts. If you need to keep $10,000 in checking to avoid a $25 monthly fee, you're essentially being charged for not having enough money. That same $10,000 in a high-yield savings account would earn real interest — and you can usually avoid checking fees entirely by choosing a different bank.

Promotional sign-up bonuses can be worthwhile, but read the fine print. A $300 bonus often requires a $5,000 direct deposit within 60 days and keeping the account open for six months. If you can't meet those conditions, the bonus disappears. Bonuses are a fine tiebreaker between two good accounts, but a bad account doesn't become good because it pays you $200 to open it.

FDIC Insurance: How It Actually Works

FDIC insurance covers $250,000 per depositor, per insured bank, per ownership category. A married couple with a joint account is insured up to $500,000 in that single account because each spouse counts separately. If you have $300,000 in deposits and want full coverage, you can either split it across two FDIC-insured banks or use different ownership categories (individual, joint, retirement) at the same bank.

Credit unions are covered by a similar program through the NCUA (National Credit Union Administration), with the same $250,000 limit. The protection works the same way. If you're considering a credit union, verify NCUA membership the same way you'd verify FDIC for a bank.

How Many Accounts Should You Have?

For most people, a clean setup is one checking account and one high-yield savings account. Add a second savings account if you have a specific medium-term goal — a house down payment, a wedding, a planned big purchase — and want to keep that money mentally separate from your emergency fund.

More than three or four accounts usually creates complexity without benefit. You lose track of balances, miss fees, forget logins, and make budgeting harder. If you find yourself opening accounts for every minor goal, the problem may be that you need a better budgeting system, not more accounts. Your Emergency Fund Belongs in Savings

Most financial guidance suggests an emergency fund covering three to six months of essential expenses. That money should sit in a high-yield savings account — accessible within one to three business days, but not so accessible that you spend it. Keeping it in checking means losing the interest growth that helps offset inflation. Keeping it in stocks or other investments means risking that the money won't be there when you actually need it.

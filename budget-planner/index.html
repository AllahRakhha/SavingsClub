50/30/20 Budget Planner

See how your income should be split between needs, wants, and savings.

What Is the 50/30/20 Budget Rule?

The 50/30/20 rule is a simple budgeting framework that divides your after-tax income into three categories: 50% for needs, 30% for wants, and 20% for savings and debt repayment. It was popularized by Senator Elizabeth Warren in her book "All Your Worth."

Needs (50%)

Needs are expenses you cannot avoid: housing, groceries, utilities, transportation, insurance premiums, and minimum debt payments. If your needs exceed 50% of income, look at housing costs first — experts recommend keeping housing below 30% of gross income. In high-cost cities like San Francisco, New York, or Boston, this may require roommates or living further from the center.

Wants (30%)

Wants include dining out, entertainment, streaming subscriptions, hobbies, and non-essential shopping. This category prevents budget burnout. Americans in cities with lower costs of living — like Austin, Nashville, or Raleigh — often find this category stretches further than coastal cities.

Savings and Debt Repayment (20%)

This is where wealth building happens: emergency fund contributions, retirement savings, extra debt payments above minimums, and investment contributions. Even if you can only start with 10%, the habit matters more than the percentage. Increase by 1% each month until you hit 20% or more.

When the 50/30/20 Rule Doesn't Work

If you live in an expensive metro area, your needs may consume 60-70% of income. In that case, consider the 60/20/20 or 70/20/10 split as a starting point. The goal is progress, not perfection. Use this budget planner to see where your money actually goes.

Frequently Asked Questions

What counts as a need vs a want?

Needs are essential for survival and basic functioning: rent, groceries, utilities, basic transportation, insurance, and minimum debt payments. Wants are everything else: dining out, entertainment, subscriptions, and upgrades.

What if my needs are more than 50%?

Many Americans, especially in high-cost cities, spend more than 50% on needs. Start by tracking your actual spending, then look for ways to reduce the biggest category — usually housing or transportation.

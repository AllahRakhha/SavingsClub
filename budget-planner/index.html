How to use this budget planner

Six steps to see where your money actually goes.

  1. Enter your after-tax income. Use the figure that actually reaches your account, not your gross salary. Not sure of the number? Our paycheck calculator works it out for you.
  2. Fill in your real Needs spending. Pull up last month's bank statement and copy the true figures for housing, groceries, transport, utilities, insurance, and minimum debt payments. Guessing defeats the point.
  3. Fill in your real Wants spending. Dining out, entertainment, subscriptions, shopping. This is the category almost everyone underestimates, so be honest with yourself.
  4. Fill in savings and debt payoff. Whatever you actually moved into savings, retirement, or extra debt payments above the minimum. If the honest answer is nothing, put zero.
  5. Pick your framework. 50/30/20 fits most people. 70/20/10 suits high-rent cities where housing takes a bigger bite. 60/20/20 sits in between and leans harder on savings.
  6. Press Calculate and read the results. You get a health rating, a bar for every category showing how far off target you are, and next steps built from your own numbers.

Understanding the 50/30/20 budget rule

The 50/30/20 rule divides your take-home pay into three buckets: half for needs, roughly a third for wants, and a fifth for savings and debt. Its strength is not precision, it is that people actually stick with three buckets. Most budgets collapse under their own detail, not from a lack of willpower. The budgeting formulas behind it are deliberately simple so you can run them in your head.

Needs, roughly half your pay

Needs are the bills that keep the lights on and a roof overhead: rent or mortgage, groceries, utilities, getting to work, insurance, and the minimum on any debt. When this bucket runs past half your income, housing is almost always the reason, since it is the single biggest line for most households. A rough ceiling many people aim for is keeping rent or mortgage near a third of income, since going well above that leaves too little room for everything else.

Wants, roughly a third

Wants are the discretionary side of life: eating out, streaming, hobbies, the occasional splurge. It is tempting to zero this bucket out, but a joyless budget rarely survives past the first month. The point is to see the number clearly, not to erase it. Add up a single month of small purchases and the total usually surprises people, which is exactly why the awareness matters more than the restriction.

Savings and debt, roughly a fifth

This is the bucket that changes your trajectory: an emergency fund, retirement contributions, investments, and any debt payment above the minimum. If a fifth feels out of reach right now, do not force it. Nudge it up a single percentage point each month. A savings rate you can hold for a year beats an ambitious one you abandon in week three, because it is consistency that compounds, not enthusiasm.

This planner does more than add up columns. When a different split makes sense

70/20/10 earns its place in expensive cities where rent genuinely takes a bigger bite. It accepts the higher housing cost and protects savings by squeezing wants instead. 60/20/20 keeps savings ambitious while giving needs a little more breathing room than the classic rule. Switch between them with the toggle at the top of the calculator and watch your targets move in real time.

When high-interest debt comes first

Credit card debt above roughly 7 percent APR usually deserves priority over investing, because clearing a 24 percent balance is a guaranteed 24 percent return, and no investment can promise that. Point your savings bucket at the debt first, then rebuild once it is gone. Budget planner questions people ask

What counts as a need versus a want?
A need is anything you cannot stop paying for without real consequences: housing, groceries, utilities, basic transport, insurance, and the minimum on your debts. Everything else is a want, from takeout to streaming to the upgrade you did not strictly require. A quick gut check is to picture losing your income next week. The bills you would still have to pay are needs. The rest are wants.
What if my needs are more than half my income?
You are far from alone, especially in high-cost cities where rent alone can swallow half a paycheck. Begin by logging what you actually spend for a month, then attack the biggest line, which is usually housing or transport. You can also switch this budget planner to the 70/20/10 framework, which allows a larger housing share while still ring-fencing your savings.
Should I budget with gross or net income?
Use net income, the amount that actually lands in your account after tax and deductions. Budgeting off your gross salary sets targets against money you never touch. If you are unsure what your take-home actually is, our paycheck calculator works it out from your gross pay.
What if my income is different every month?
If you freelance, work gig shifts, or earn commission, budget against the lowest monthly income you have seen in the past six months and treat that as your baseline. Anything a stronger month brings in goes straight to savings or debt. That way an unpredictable income turns into a plan you can actually rely on.

Can two people share one budget planner?
Yes, and combining incomes and bills into a single view usually works better than budgeting separately. Pool your take-home pay, list every shared cost once, and run the 50/30/20 split on the household total. The Excel and Sheets download doubles as a couples monthly budget template, and it is one of the more useful budget templates for couples precisely because both people see the same numbers.
How is the 50/30/20 rule different from envelope budgeting?
The 50/30/20 rule works from the top down, splitting your income into three broad buckets. Envelope budgeting works from the bottom up, assigning a fixed amount to many small categories and stopping once each envelope is empty. Neither is wrong. If you want tighter control you can build your own budget planner around envelopes, or use an envelope budgeting spreadsheet, but most people find three buckets far easier to sustain.

How to calculate CD interest

A certificate of deposit earns compound interest, which means the interest you earn starts earning interest of its own. The stated annual rate gets split into smaller periods, and each one builds on the last. The formula behind the CD interest calculator above is this:

Maturity = Deposit × (1 + r / n) ^ (n × t)

Here r is the annual rate written as a decimal, n is how many times per year interest compounds, and t is the term in years. To get the interest earned on its own, subtract your original deposit from the maturity value.

A worked example, step by step

Say you put 10,000 dollars into a 12-month CD at a 4.5 percent rate, compounded monthly.

  1. Convert the rate to a decimal. 4.5 percent becomes 0.045, so r = 0.045.
  2. Set the compounding. Monthly means twelve times a year, so n = 12.
  3. Set the term in years. Twelve months is one year, so t = 1.
  4. Apply the formula. 10,000 times (1 + 0.045 / 12) raised to the power of 12 equals 10,459.40 dollars at maturity.
  5. Subtract the deposit. 10,459.40 minus 10,000 leaves 459.40 dollars of interest earned.

That 459.40 dollars is a 4.59 percent APY, not 4.5 percent, because compounding lifted the effective yield slightly above the stated rate. Enter those same numbers in the calculator above and it lands on the identical figure.

CD interest compounded monthly versus daily

This is the question that trips people up, and the honest answer will save you some worry. Take the same 10,000 dollar CD at 4.5 percent for one year:

CompoundingInterest earnedEffective APY
Annually$450.004.50%
Monthly$459.404.59%
Daily$460.254.60%

Daily compounding beats monthly by 85 cents over a full year on 10,000 dollars. That is the entire prize. Banks advertise daily compounding as though it were a meaningful advantage, and mathematically it does earn more, but the gap is a rounding error next to the rate itself. A CD paying 4.75 percent compounded annually will crush a CD paying 4.5 percent compounded daily every single time.

Here is the rule that follows from that, and it is the single most useful thing on this page: compare CDs by APY, never by compounding frequency. The APY already includes the compounding. If two CDs advertise the same APY, they pay exactly the same amount regardless of how often they compound.

How CD interest is paid out

On most CDs the interest is credited to the account on a schedule, often monthly or quarterly, but it stays inside the CD and compounds until the term ends. You do not receive money in your checking account each month. That is exactly what makes the compounding work.

Some banks do offer a monthly interest payout option, where the interest is transferred to you as it is earned. It sounds appealing if you want income, but understand the trade: money paid out cannot compound, so your total return over the term is lower. Take the payout only if you actually need the cash flow.

CD interest inside an IRA

The interest math on an IRA CD is identical to a regular CD, so the calculator above works for both. What changes is tax. Interest on a regular CD is taxed as ordinary income in the year it is credited, even though you cannot touch the money yet. Interest on a CD held inside a traditional IRA grows tax-deferred, and inside a Roth IRA it can grow tax-free. For conservative retirement money, that difference compounds into real value over time.

What CD rate to expect in 2026

As of 2026, competitive CD rates run roughly 4 to 4.75 percent APY, and online banks and credit unions usually beat the big national banks by a wide margin. CD rates track the Federal Reserve benchmark rate: when the Fed cuts, newly issued CD rates fall, and when it holds or raises, they stay high. Because a CD locks its rate at purchase, opening one while rates are high keeps that yield for the full term even if rates drop later. That is the main reason to choose a CD over a savings account whose rate can fall at any time.

What an early withdrawal really costs

If you break a CD before it matures, most banks charge a penalty equal to a set number of months of interest, commonly 3 to 12 months depending on the term. On a short CD, or one you cash out early in the term, that penalty can wipe out most of your earnings and in some cases dip into your original deposit. The calculator above estimates this for your inputs. No-penalty CDs avoid the problem but usually pay a slightly lower rate, so they suit money you think you might need. This is also why your emergency fund belongs in savings, not in a CD.

CD versus high-yield savings account

A CD locks in a guaranteed rate for a fixed term, but you cannot touch the money without a penalty. A high-yield savings account keeps your money available at any time, but the rate can change whenever the bank decides. If you can set the money aside for the full term, a CD often pays a little more and removes the risk of falling rates. If you might need the cash, keep it in high-yield savings instead. Both are insured by the FDIC up to 250,000 dollars per depositor per bank.

CD laddering, in one paragraph

Instead of locking everything into one CD, a ladder splits your money across several CDs that mature at staggered dates, for example 3, 6, 12, and 24 months. As each one matures you roll it into a new longer-term CD. You get regular access to a slice of your money while still capturing higher long-term rates, and you are never fully locked in. Types of CDs, and when each one makes sense

Not every CD is the plain vanilla kind. Knowing the variations helps you avoid locking into the wrong one.

No-penalty CD. Lets you withdraw early without forfeiting interest, in exchange for a slightly lower rate. Good for money you probably will not touch but might.

Bump-up CD. Lets you raise your rate once during the term if the bank increases its rates. Useful when rates look likely to climb, though it usually starts a little lower.

Jumbo CD. Requires a large deposit, often 100,000 dollars or more, sometimes for a small rate bump. The extra yield is usually thin, so compare it against a standard CD before assuming bigger is better.

Brokered CD. Bought through a brokerage rather than a bank. It can offer higher rates and can be sold before maturity, but selling early can lose value if rates have risen, and it behaves differently from a bank CD.

IRA CD. A CD held inside a retirement account, so the interest grows tax-deferred or tax-free. Suited to conservative retirement money, not cash you might need soon.

How to open a CD, step by step

  1. Compare APYs across several online banks and credit unions, not just your current bank. Online banks usually pay far more than big national branches.
  2. Pick a term that matches when you will actually need the money, not simply the highest rate on the board.
  3. Read the early withdrawal penalty before you sign, and note whether it is a no-penalty CD.
  4. Fund the CD, usually by transfer from a linked checking or savings account.
  5. Write down the maturity date and set a reminder, because most CDs auto-renew if you do nothing.
  6. At maturity, decide whether to withdraw or let it roll into a new term at the current rate.

Frequently Asked Questions

How is CD interest calculated?

CD interest is compound interest. The maturity value is your deposit times (1 plus r divided by n) raised to the power of n times t, where r is the annual rate as a decimal, n is how many times per year interest compounds, and t is the term in years. For example, 10,000 dollars at a 4.5 percent rate compounded monthly for 12 months grows to 10,459.40 dollars, so you earn 459.40 dollars in interest.

How do you calculate interest earned on a CD?

Work out the maturity value first, then subtract your original deposit. The difference is the interest earned. On a 10,000 dollar CD at 4.5 percent compounded monthly for one year, the maturity value is 10,459.40 dollars, so the interest earned is 459.40 dollars. The calculator above does both steps for you.

How much interest does a 10,000 dollar CD earn?

At a 4.5 percent rate compounded monthly, a 10,000 dollar CD earns 459.40 dollars in one year, which is a 4.59 percent APY. Over three years at the same rate it earns 1,442.48 dollars, and over five years it earns 2,517.96 dollars. The exact figure depends on your rate, term, and compounding.

Is CD interest compounded monthly or daily?

It varies by bank. Both are common, and the difference is far smaller than most people expect. On a 10,000 dollar CD at 4.5 percent for one year, monthly compounding earns 459.40 dollars and daily compounding earns 460.25 dollars. That is a difference of 85 cents. Compare CDs by APY, not by how often they compound.

Does a CD pay interest monthly?

Interest is usually credited to the CD on a set schedule, often monthly or quarterly, but on most CDs it stays inside the account and compounds until maturity rather than being paid out to you. Some banks let you take the interest as a monthly payout instead, though that stops it compounding and lowers your total return.

What is the difference between APR and APY on a CD?

APR is the stated annual rate before compounding. APY is the effective yield after compounding is applied, so APY is what you actually earn in a year. Banks advertise the APY. A 4.5 percent rate compounded monthly produces an APY of 4.59 percent.

Can I use this as an IRA CD interest calculator?

Yes. The interest math is identical for a CD held inside an IRA. The difference is tax treatment, not calculation. Interest on a regular CD is taxed as ordinary income each year, while a CD held inside an IRA grows tax-deferred, or tax-free in a Roth IRA.

Are CDs worth it in 2026?

With competitive CD rates around 4 to 4.75 percent APY, a CD is worth it for money you will not need until the term ends and want a guaranteed, fixed return. If you might need the cash sooner, a high-yield savings account is usually the better fit because it stays liquid.

What happens if I withdraw from a CD early?

Most banks charge an early withdrawal penalty, commonly 3 to 12 months of interest depending on the term. The penalty can reduce or even erase your earnings, and in some cases eat into your principal. No-penalty CDs exist but usually pay a slightly lower rate.

Can I lose money in a CD?

Not if you hold it to maturity at an FDIC-insured bank, where deposits are protected up to 250,000 dollars per depositor per bank. The common way to lose money is an early withdrawal penalty larger than the interest you earned, which can dip into your principal. Brokered CDs sold before maturity can also lose value if rates have risen.

Is CD interest taxed?

Yes. CD interest is taxed as ordinary income in the year it is credited, even if the CD has not matured yet. Your bank sends a Form 1099-INT if you earn 10 dollars or more in interest. CDs held inside an IRA are tax-advantaged.

Should I put my emergency fund in a CD?

Usually not. An emergency fund needs to be available the day you need it, and a CD locks your money for the full term with a penalty for early access. A high-yield savings account is the standard home for an emergency fund. Size yours with our emergency fund calculator first, then consider a CD only for money beyond that cushion.

CD versus high-yield savings account, which is better?

A CD locks a guaranteed rate for a fixed term but restricts access to your money. A high-yield savings account keeps your money liquid but its rate can change at any time. Choose a CD for money you can set aside for the full term, and a high-yield savings account for money you may need sooner. Both are FDIC insured up to 250,000 dollars per depositor per bank.

What is a CD ladder?

A CD ladder splits your money across several CDs with staggered maturity dates, such as 3, 6, 12, and 24 months. As each CD matures you reinvest it into a new longer-term CD. This gives you regular access to a portion of your money while still capturing higher long-term rates.

The certificate of deposit formula, in plain terms

A certificate of deposit pays compound interest: each slice of interest you earn is folded back in and starts earning on its own. The advertised annual rate is chopped into smaller periods, and every period stacks on the one before it. Written out, the certificate of deposit formula the calculator above uses looks like this:

Maturity = Deposit × (1 + r / n) ^ (n × t)

In that equation r is the yearly rate as a decimal, n is the number of times a year the interest compounds, and t is the term measured in years. Want the interest on its own? Take the maturity value and subtract the money you put in. That single subtraction is the whole of how to calculate interest earned on a CD.

A worked example, one step at a time

Picture 10,000 dollars going into a 12-month CD at a 4.5 percent rate, compounding monthly.

  1. Turn the rate into a decimal. 4.5 percent is 0.045, so r = 0.045.
  2. Count the compounding periods. Monthly is twelve a year, so n = 12.
  3. State the term in years. Twelve months is one year, so t = 1.
  4. Run the equation. 10,000 times (1 + 0.045 / 12) to the power of 12 comes out to 10,459.40 dollars at maturity.
  5. Strip out your deposit. 10,459.40 minus 10,000 leaves 459.40 dollars earned.

Notice the yield lands at 4.59 percent APY rather than the stated 4.5 percent, because monthly compounding nudged the effective return a hair higher. Feed those same inputs into the tool above and it settles on the very same figure.

How much a CD earns per month

People often want the monthly number, not just the yearly one. Here is the honest picture: on a 10,000 dollar CD at 4.5 percent, the first month credits roughly 37.50 dollars (10,000 times 0.045 divided by 12). Each following month is fractionally larger, because it earns on the slightly bigger balance, and across the year those twelve deposits add up to the 459.40 dollars above rather than a flat 37.50 times 12. That is compounding doing quiet work in the background. If your bank offers a monthly payout instead of leaving the interest inside, you would receive close to that 37.50 dollars each month, but the balance stops growing, so the yearly total lands a little lower.

Monthly versus daily compounding: the 85-cent question

This is the detail that snags people, and the plain answer should put your mind at ease. Take the same 10,000 dollar CD at 4.5 percent for one year:

Annually$450.004.50%

Daily compounding tops monthly by all of 85 cents over a full year on 10,000 dollars. That is the entire prize. Marketing loves to wave daily compounding around, and yes, it technically wins, but the margin is a rounding error beside the rate itself. A CD paying 4.75 percent compounded annually beats a 4.5 percent CD compounded daily every single time.

So here is the rule worth carrying away, the single most useful line on this page: compare CDs by APY, never by how often they compound. The APY has the compounding already baked in. Two CDs quoting the same APY pay identical amounts, full stop.

When the interest actually lands in your hands

On the typical CD, interest is credited on a schedule, monthly or quarterly, but it stays parked inside the certificate and keeps compounding right up to maturity. Nothing shows up in your checking along the way, and that is precisely what lets the compounding build.

Some banks bolt on a monthly payout option that sends the interest to you as it accrues. Tempting if you want the income, but weigh the cost: money paid out cannot compound, so your total over the term shrinks. Reach for the payout only when the cash flow genuinely matters to you now.

Running the numbers on an IRA CD

The arithmetic on an IRA CD matches a regular CD exactly, so this tool works as an IRA CD interest calculator without any change. Tax is the only moving part. A standard CD is taxed as ordinary income the year the interest posts, even while the money is still locked. Inside a traditional IRA that interest grows tax-deferred, and inside a Roth IRA it can grow tax-free. For steady, conservative retirement cash, that gap compounds into something real.

Share certificates: the credit-union version of a CD

If you bank with a credit union, you may see the word "share certificate" or "term share certificate" instead of CD. It is the same product under a different name: a fixed rate for a fixed term, backed by NCUA insurance rather than FDIC, but insured to the same 250,000 dollars per member. The math on this page applies identically, so use it as a savings certificate calculator too. Credit unions frequently post some of the strongest rates around, so it is worth pricing a share certificate against the bank CDs on your shortlist.

What breaking a CD early really costs

Cash out before maturity and most banks levy a penalty set as a fixed number of months of interest, commonly 3 to 12 depending on the term. On a short CD, or one you crack open early in its life, that hit can erase most of the earnings and occasionally chew into the deposit. The tool above works as a quick certificate of deposit penalty calculator, estimating the damage for your inputs. No-penalty CDs dodge the issue but usually shave a little off the rate, so they fit money you suspect you might need. It is also the reason your emergency fund belongs in savings rather than tied up in a certificate.

CD or high-yield savings: matching the tool to the money

A CD pins down a guaranteed rate for a fixed stretch, but the money is off-limits without a penalty. A high-yield savings account stays reachable whenever you like, though the rate can move the moment the bank decides. Money you can genuinely set aside for the full term usually earns a touch more in a CD and shrugs off the risk of falling rates. Money you might need should sit in high-yield savings. Both come wrapped in FDIC insurance up to 250,000 dollars per depositor per bank.

What CD rate to expect in 2026

Through 2026, competitive CD rates sit roughly between 4 and 4.75 percent APY, with online banks and credit unions routinely outpacing the big brick-and-mortar names. These rates shadow the Federal Reserve benchmark: when the Fed trims, freshly issued CDs slide with it, and when it holds firm, they hold too. Because a CD freezes its rate on the day you open it, locking one in while rates are elevated preserves that yield for the whole term even if the market turns lower later. That is the core case for a CD over a savings account whose rate can drift down whenever.

Building a CD ladder

Rather than sink everything into a single certificate, a ladder divides the money across several CDs that mature on staggered dates, say 3, 6, 12, and 24 months. As each rung comes due you roll it into a fresh, longer one. The payoff is regular access to a portion of your cash paired with the higher yields of longer terms, and you are never wholly locked in. The main flavors of CD

Not every certificate is the plain kind. Knowing the variants keeps you out of the wrong one.

No-penalty CD. Withdraw early without surrendering interest, traded for a slightly lower rate. Handy for money you likely will not touch but just might.

Bump-up CD. Lets you lift your rate once mid-term if the bank raises theirs. Worth it when rates look set to climb, though it usually opens a little lower.

Jumbo CD. Wants a large deposit, often 100,000 dollars or more, occasionally for a modest rate bump. The extra yield tends to be slim, so measure it against a standard CD before assuming bigger wins.

Brokered CD. Purchased through a brokerage rather than a bank. It can carry higher rates and can be sold before maturity, but an early sale can lose value once rates rise, and it behaves unlike a bank CD.

IRA CD. A certificate held inside a retirement account, so the interest grows tax-deferred or tax-free. Suited to conservative retirement money, not cash you might need soon.

Opening a CD without the common mistakes

  1. Compare APYs across a handful of online banks and credit unions, not just your current one. Online outfits usually pay far more than big national branches.
  2. Choose a term tied to when you will genuinely need the money, not simply the flashiest rate on the board.
  3. Read the early withdrawal penalty before signing, and note whether it is a no-penalty CD.
  4. Fund it, usually by transfer from a linked checking or savings account.
  5. Record the maturity date and set a reminder, since most CDs auto-renew if you do nothing.
  6. At maturity, decide deliberately whether to withdraw or let it roll into a new term at the going rate.

Frequently Asked Questions

How is CD interest calculated?

A CD uses compound interest. Multiply your deposit by (1 plus r divided by n) raised to the power of n times t, where r is the yearly rate as a decimal, n is the number of compounding periods per year, and t is the term in years. Put 10,000 dollars in a 12-month CD at 4.5 percent compounded monthly and it grows to 10,459.40 dollars, which is 459.40 dollars of interest. That certificate of deposit formula is exactly what the tool above runs.

How do you calculate interest earned on a CD?

Find the maturity value first, then subtract what you deposited; the remainder is your interest earned. A 10,000 dollar CD at 4.5 percent compounded monthly matures at 10,459.40 dollars, so 459.40 dollars is interest. If you would rather skip the arithmetic, the calculator above does both steps for you.

How much interest does a 10,000 dollar CD earn?

At 4.5 percent compounded monthly, 10,000 dollars earns 459.40 dollars in a year, an effective 4.59 percent APY. Hold the same rate for three years and it earns 1,442.48 dollars; over five years, 2,517.96 dollars. Your own number shifts with the rate, term, and compounding you pick.

Is CD interest compounded monthly or daily?

It depends on the bank, and it barely matters. On a 10,000 dollar CD at 4.5 percent for one year, monthly compounding pays 459.40 dollars and daily pays 460.25 dollars, a gap of 85 cents. Judge CDs by their APY, which already includes the compounding, not by how often they compound.

Does a CD pay interest monthly?

On most CDs the interest posts on a set schedule, often monthly or quarterly, but it stays locked inside the account compounding until the term ends rather than landing in your checking. A few banks offer a monthly payout option that hands you the interest as income, though that stops it compounding and lowers your total return.

What is the difference between APR and APY on a CD?

APR is the plain annual rate before any compounding. APY is what you actually keep once compounding is counted, which is why banks quote the APY. A 4.5 percent rate compounded monthly works out to a 4.59 percent APY.

Can I use this as an IRA CD interest calculator?

Yes. The math for a CD held inside an IRA is identical, so this doubles as an IRA CD interest calculator. Only the tax treatment differs: a regular CD is taxed as income each year, while an IRA CD grows tax-deferred, or entirely tax-free inside a Roth IRA.

Are CDs worth it in 2026?

With top CD rates near 4 to 4.75 percent APY, a CD earns its keep for money you can leave untouched until maturity and want locked at a guaranteed rate. If there is a real chance you will need the cash early, a high-yield savings account fits better because it stays reachable.

What happens if I withdraw from a CD early?

Break the term early and most banks dock an early withdrawal penalty, usually 3 to 12 months of interest depending on the length. That charge can swallow your earnings and sometimes bite into the deposit itself. A no-penalty CD avoids it but tends to pay slightly less.

Can I lose money in a CD?

Held to maturity at an FDIC-insured bank, no; deposits are covered to 250,000 dollars per depositor per bank. The usual way people lose money is an early withdrawal penalty that outruns the interest earned and eats into principal. Brokered CDs sold before maturity can also slip in value when rates have risen.

Is CD interest taxed?

Yes. The IRS treats CD interest as ordinary income in the year it is credited, even on a CD that has not matured yet, and your bank issues a Form 1099-INT once you earn 10 dollars or more. A CD held inside an IRA is the exception, since it is tax-advantaged.

Should I put my emergency fund in a CD?

Generally no. An emergency fund has to be reachable the moment you need it, and a CD ties the money up for the whole term with a penalty for early access. Keep that cash in a high-yield savings account instead, and save CDs for money that sits beyond your safety cushion.

CD versus high-yield savings account, which is better?

A CD nails down a guaranteed rate for a set term but fences off your money. A high-yield savings account leaves the cash open while its rate floats with the market. Pick the CD for money you can commit for the full term, and high-yield savings for anything you might touch sooner. Both carry FDIC coverage to 250,000 dollars per depositor per bank.

What is a CD ladder?

A CD ladder spreads your money across several CDs that come due at different times, say 3, 6, 12, and 24 months. Each time one matures you roll it into a fresh longer rung. You keep steady access to part of your cash while still locking in the better long-term rates.

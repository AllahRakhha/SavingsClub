How CD interest is calculated

A certificate of deposit earns compound interest. The stated annual rate is divided into smaller pieces, and each piece earns interest on top of the previous ones. The maturity value follows this formula:

Maturity = Deposit × (1 + r / n) ^ (n × t)

Here r is the annual interest rate as a decimal, n is how many times per year interest compounds, and t is the term in years. The APY, or annual percentage yield, is the effective yearly return after compounding: APY = (1 + r / n) ^ n minus 1.

Worked example. Put 10,000 dollars in a 12-month CD at a 4.5 percent rate compounded monthly. Then r is 0.045, n is 12, and t is 1. The APY works out to about 4.59 percent, and the maturity value is about 10,459 dollars, so you earn roughly 459 dollars in interest. Enter those same numbers in the CD rates calculator above and it confirms it.

What CD rate to expect in 2026

As of 2026, competitive CD rates run roughly 4 to 4.75 percent APY, and online banks and credit unions usually beat the big national banks by a wide margin. CD rates track the Federal Reserve benchmark rate: when the Fed cuts, newly issued CD rates fall, and when it holds or raises, they stay high. Because a CD locks its rate at purchase, opening one while rates are high keeps that yield for the full term even if rates drop later. That is the main reason to use a CD instead of a savings account whose rate can fall at any time. APR versus APY, and why compounding barely matters once you know the APY

APR is the plain stated rate before compounding. APY is what you actually earn after compounding, which is why banks advertise the APY. For the same stated rate, compounding more often earns slightly more, because your interest starts earning interest sooner. But here is the part most calculators hide: if two CDs both advertise the same APY, they pay exactly the same amount no matter how often they compound, because the APY already bakes the compounding in. So compare CDs by APY, not by compounding frequency.

CD versus high-yield savings account

A CD locks in a guaranteed rate for a fixed term, but you cannot touch the money without a penalty. A high-yield savings account keeps your money available at any time, but the rate can change whenever the bank decides. If you can set the money aside for the full term, a CD often pays a little more and removes the risk of falling rates. If you might need the cash, keep it in a high-yield savings account instead. Both are insured by the FDIC up to 250,000 dollars per depositor per bank.

What an early withdrawal really costs

If you break a CD before it matures, most banks charge a penalty equal to a set number of months of interest, commonly 3 to 12 months depending on the term. On a short CD or one you cash out early, that penalty can wipe out most of your earnings, and in some cases dip into your original deposit. The estimate above shows the typical penalty for your inputs. No-penalty CDs avoid this but usually pay a slightly lower rate, so they suit money you think you might need. This is also why your emergency fund belongs in savings, not a CD.

CD laddering, in one paragraph

Instead of locking everything into one CD, a ladder splits your money across several CDs that mature at staggered dates, for example 3, 6, 12, and 24 months. As each one matures you roll it into a new longer-term CD. You get regular access to a slice of your money while still capturing higher long-term rates, and you are never fully locked in. Frequently Asked Questions

How is CD interest calculated?

CD interest uses compound interest. The maturity value is your deposit times (1 plus r divided by n) raised to the power of n times t, where r is the annual interest rate, n is how many times per year interest compounds, and t is the term in years. For example, 10,000 dollars at a 4.5 percent rate compounded monthly for 12 months grows to about 10,459 dollars, or 459 dollars of interest.

What is the difference between APR and APY on a CD?

APR is the stated annual rate before compounding. APY is the effective yield after compounding is applied, so APY is what you actually earn in a year. Banks advertise the APY. A 4.5 percent rate compounded monthly produces an APY of about 4.59 percent.

Does more frequent compounding earn more on a CD?

For the same stated rate, yes, but only slightly. Monthly compounding earns a little more than annual because interest starts earning interest sooner. However, if two CDs advertise the same APY, they pay exactly the same regardless of how often they compound, because the APY already includes the compounding.

How much does a 10,000 dollar CD earn?

It depends on the rate and term. At a 4.5 percent rate compounded monthly, a 10,000 dollar CD earns about 459 dollars in one year, which is a 4.59 percent APY. Over 3 years at the same rate it earns about 1,442 dollars. Enter your own rate and term in the calculator above for the exact figure.

Are CDs worth it in 2026?

With competitive CD rates around 4 to 4.75 percent APY, a CD is worth it for money you will not need until the term ends and want a guaranteed, fixed return. If you might need the cash sooner, a high-yield savings account is usually the better fit because it stays liquid.

What happens if I withdraw from a CD early?

Most banks charge an early withdrawal penalty, commonly 3 to 12 months of interest depending on the term. The penalty can reduce or even erase your earnings, and in some cases eat into your principal. No-penalty CDs exist but usually pay a slightly lower rate.

Can I lose money in a CD?

Not if you hold it to maturity at an FDIC-insured bank, where deposits are protected up to 250,000 dollars per depositor per bank. The common way to lose money is an early withdrawal penalty that is larger than the interest you earned, which can dip into your principal. Brokered CDs sold before maturity can also lose value if rates have risen.

Is CD interest taxed?

Yes. CD interest is taxed as ordinary income in the year it is credited, even if the CD has not matured yet. Your bank sends a Form 1099-INT if you earn 10 dollars or more in interest. CDs held inside an IRA are tax-advantaged.

Should I put my emergency fund in a CD?

Usually not. An emergency fund needs to be available the day you need it, and a CD locks your money for the full term with a penalty for early access. A high-yield savings account is the standard home for an emergency fund. Size yours with our emergency fund calculator first, then consider a CD only for money beyond that cushion.

CD versus high-yield savings account, which is better?

A CD locks a guaranteed rate for a fixed term but restricts access to your money. A high-yield savings account keeps your money liquid but its rate can change at any time. Choose a CD for money you can set aside for the full term, and a high-yield savings account for money you may need sooner. Both are FDIC insured up to 250,000 dollars per depositor per bank.

What is the best CD term for me?

Match the term to when you will need the money, not to the highest rate. A short term of 3 to 12 months keeps you flexible if rates rise or you need the cash. A longer term of 2 to 5 years locks in today's rate, which is valuable when rates are expected to fall. If you are unsure, a CD ladder splits the difference.

What is a CD ladder?

A CD ladder splits your money across several CDs with staggered maturity dates, such as 3, 6, 12, and 24 months. As each CD matures you reinvest it into a new longer-term CD. This gives you regular access to a portion of your money while capturing higher long-term rates.

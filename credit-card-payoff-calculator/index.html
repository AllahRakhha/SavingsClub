SavingsClub Tips
Three things most people miss when paying off credit cards
1
Pay biweekly, not monthly. Credit card interest is calculated daily, not monthly. Splitting your payment in half and paying every two weeks lowers your average daily balance and cuts your interest noticeably over the payoff period — without you spending an extra dollar.
2
Call and ask for a lower APR. Issuers grant APR reductions more often than people expect, especially for customers in good standing. Even a 3-percentage-point drop on a $5,000 balance saves over $150 per year. It costs you a 10-minute phone call. The worst they can say is no.
3
Snowflake every windfall. Tax refunds, side gig money, bonuses, gift cash — apply small extra payments ("snowflakes") on top of your regular plan. A $200 snowflake every couple of months on a multi-year payoff can save hundreds in interest and shave months off the timeline.

How credit card interest actually works

Credit cards charge interest daily, not monthly. Your APR is divided by 365 to get a daily rate, and that rate is applied to your balance every day you carry one. By the time you see your statement, interest has compounded all month. This is why a "22% APR" actually costs you closer to 24-25% effective annual rate when you do nothing but pay the minimum.

The minimum payment trap works like this: most issuers calculate the minimum as interest plus about 1% of your principal balance, with a floor of $25. On a $5,000 balance at 22% APR, that's roughly $140 per month. Of that, about $92 goes to interest. Only $48 actually reduces your balance. At that rate, paying only the minimum on a $5,000 balance takes nearly 19 years and costs more than $7,700 in interest — more than the original debt.

Avalanche vs Snowball, with real math

These are the two most-discussed debt payoff methods. They're not the same.

Avalanche targets the highest APR card first while paying minimums on the rest. Once the highest-APR card is paid off, you roll that payment into the next-highest-APR card. This minimizes total interest paid.

Snowball targets the smallest balance first regardless of APR. When that card is paid off, you get a quick win — one less bill — and roll that payment into the next-smallest balance. This creates momentum and is psychologically easier to sustain.

Example: three cards totaling $12,000 ($1,000 at 22%, $3,000 at 18%, $8,000 at 26%) with $400/month total payment.

Under avalanche (highest APR first, $8,000 card targeted), you pay it off in roughly 47 months with about $6,100 in total interest. Under snowball (smallest balance first, $1,000 card targeted), you pay it off in roughly 48 months with about $6,800 in total interest. The avalanche saves around $650 with about the same finish time on this specific mix.

The math favors avalanche, but the better strategy is the one you'll actually follow. A 2016 study by Northwestern's Kellogg School found that people who used the snowball method were more likely to stay engaged and pay off their debts than those who used the rational avalanche approach. Pick the one you'll stick with.

When a balance transfer makes sense

A balance transfer card moves debt from a high-APR card to a new card with a 0% promotional APR, usually for 12 to 21 months. You pay a transfer fee (typically 3% to 5% of the amount transferred), but if you can pay off the entire balance before the promotional rate ends, you save substantially on interest.

The math: transferring $8,000 from a 26% APR card to a 0% intro card with a 3% fee adds $240 in fees but eliminates all interest during the intro period. If you can pay off the full balance within the intro window, you might save $1,500 to $3,000 versus staying on the original card.

It only works if three conditions are met. First, you have to qualify, which usually requires good-to-excellent credit. Second, your monthly payment has to be high enough to clear the balance before the intro period ends. Otherwise the remainder reverts to the regular APR, often 22% or higher. Third, you can't keep using the original card. Closing it isn't necessary, but adding new debt while you're paying off the transferred amount defeats the purpose.

Common mistakes to avoid

Paying only the minimum. Even doubling the minimum payment can cut your payoff time by more than half. The calculator above shows this clearly — try setting your monthly payment to the auto-calculated minimum versus 2x the minimum and watch what happens.

Spreading payment evenly across all cards. This feels fair but it's mathematically worse than both avalanche and snowball. You make slow progress on everything and zero progress on anything.

Closing cards as you pay them off. This often hurts your credit score by reducing your available credit and shortening your credit history. Unless the card has an annual fee you can't justify, leave it open at zero balance.

Taking on new debt while paying off old debt. Lifestyle creep is the most common reason payoff plans fail. If you're using your cards while trying to pay them off, you're swimming against the current.

Ignoring the penalty APR. One missed payment can trigger a penalty APR of 29% or 30%, sometimes for six months or longer. Set up autopay for at least the minimum on every card you have, even if you plan to pay more.

Believing for-profit debt settlement companies. Companies that promise to "settle your debts for pennies on the dollar" usually charge large upfront fees, instruct you to stop paying creditors (destroying your credit), and rarely deliver the promised savings. Non-profit NFCC-accredited credit counselors are a better starting point if you can't pay on your own.

Frequently Asked Questions

How are credit card minimum payments calculated?

Most issuers calculate the minimum as the month's interest plus about 1% of your principal balance, with a floor of $25. On a $5,000 balance at 22% APR, that's typically around $140 per month — barely above the interest charge alone. Some issuers use slightly different formulas (1% to 3% of balance), but the result is similar: minimum payments are designed to keep you paying for decades.

What's the difference between avalanche and snowball methods?

Avalanche pays off the card with the highest APR first, which saves the most money in interest mathematically. Snowball pays off the smallest balance first, which creates faster wins and tends to be easier to stick with. Avalanche wins on math; snowball wins on psychology. The right choice depends on which one you'll actually follow through on.

Should I use a balance transfer card?

A balance transfer to a 0% intro APR card can save significant interest if you can pay off the full balance before the intro period ends, usually 12 to 21 months. Transfer fees are typically 3% to 5% of the amount moved. The math works only if your monthly payment is high enough to clear the balance before the promotional rate expires, otherwise you'll face the regular APR on the remainder.

Will paying off my credit cards hurt my credit score?

Paying down balances generally helps your credit score because it lowers your credit utilization ratio, which is one of the largest factors in FICO scoring. Closing a paid-off card can sometimes lower your score by reducing your available credit and shortening your credit history. Many people keep paid-off cards open with no balance for this reason.

What if I can't afford even my minimum payments?

If you can't make minimum payments, options include calling each issuer to ask about hardship programs, working with a non-profit credit counseling agency (NFCC-accredited), or in serious cases, debt management plans or settlement. Avoid for-profit debt settlement companies that charge upfront fees, and be cautious of any solution that requires you to stop paying creditors.

Should I close paid-off credit cards?

Generally no, unless the card has an annual fee you can't justify. Keeping cards open preserves your total available credit and your average account age, both of which help your credit score. If you're worried about temptation, lock the card in a drawer rather than closing the account.

How to Choose a Credit Card

Every credit card falls into one of four categories, and picking the wrong one is how a rewards card turns into a debt trap. This guide covers the four types, the three numbers that actually matter, and what carrying a balance really costs at today's rates.

The Four Credit Card Categories

Almost every card sold in the United States belongs to one of four families. Matching the family to your situation is the decision that matters; comparing individual cards only makes sense once you have done that. Get it wrong and you end up with a travel card you never fly on, or a rewards card whose interest quietly outruns the rewards.

Category 1

Cash back cards

These refund a slice of what you spend. Flat-rate versions pay the same percentage on everything, typically 1.5 to 2 percent. Category versions pay more in specific areas such as groceries, fuel, or restaurants, often 3 to 6 percent there and 1 percent elsewhere, and some rotate those categories quarterly and require you to activate them.

Best suited to: anyone who clears the statement balance every month and wants a passive return on spending they were doing regardless.

Check before applying: that a flat-rate card has no annual fee, and that the category definitions match where you actually shop. "Groceries" frequently excludes superstores, which catches people out.

The trap: rewards are cancelled out instantly by interest. A card paying 2 percent back while charging 22 percent on a $3,000 balance costs you roughly $600 a year net. The reward rate is irrelevant if you carry a balance.

Category 2

Travel rewards cards

These earn points or miles rather than cash. The stronger ones let you move points to airline and hotel partners, where the value per point can beat the cash equivalent, and add benefits such as lounge access, checked bags, travel insurance, or credits toward trusted traveler programs.

Best suited to: people who fly or stay away often enough to use the benefits, and who are willing to learn how transfer partners work. Lounge access is worth nothing to someone who flies once a year.

Check before applying: keep the annual fee under about $100 while you are learning. Premium cards charging $395 to $695 only pay off if you genuinely use the credits. Flexible points beat single-airline points.

The trap: the fee feels trivial at signup and substantial at renewal. Put a reminder in your calendar a month before each renewal date and make an active decision to keep, downgrade, or cancel.

Category 3

Credit-building cards

Built for people with no credit file, damaged credit, or a history they are recovering from. Secured cards take a refundable deposit, usually $200 to $500, which becomes the credit limit. Unsecured starter cards skip the deposit but compensate with higher fees and rates.

Best suited to: students opening a first account, new arrivals without a US credit file, and anyone rebuilding after default or bankruptcy.

Check before applying: that it reports to all three bureaus. Without that, the card builds nothing. Also look for a low or zero annual fee, a stated path to an unsecured card, and confirmation that the deposit is returned when you upgrade or close in good standing.

The trap: some cards in this category charge $75 to $150 a year plus monthly maintenance fees, which eats a $200 deposit alive. Better options exist. And none of it works without on-time payments; a single late payment can cost 60 to 100 points and stays on your file for seven years. Category 4

Balance transfer and 0% APR cards

These exist to do one job: let you clear existing card debt without interest piling up while you do it. Intro periods commonly run 12 to 21 months, applying to transfers, new purchases, or both.

Best suited to: people carrying a balance who have a realistic plan to clear it inside the intro window. Useless, and often harmful, for anyone who will keep adding new debt.

Check before applying: an intro period of at least 15 months, a transfer fee at the standard 3 percent rather than 5, no annual fee, and a tolerable rate for whatever remains afterward.

The trap: the rate snaps back to 20 percent or more the day the intro period ends. Do the arithmetic first. Clearing $5,000 across a 15-month window needs about $333 every month plus the transfer fee. If that is not affordable, a transfer postpones the problem rather than solving it. Three Numbers Every Credit Card Has

Card marketing talks about perks. The three figures below decide whether a card helps you or costs you.

APR

The annual percentage rate is what you are charged on any balance carried past the due date. Pay the statement balance in full and it never applies, which is why two people holding the identical card can have completely different experiences of it. Carry a balance and it becomes the only number that matters.

Annual fee

The flat yearly cost of holding the card. Plenty charge nothing. A fee is only defensible if the rewards and credits you genuinely use exceed it, and the test is what you actually redeemed last year, not what the card theoretically offers.

Credit limit

The ceiling on what you can borrow. A higher limit helps your score by lowering utilization, provided you do not treat it as an invitation to spend. On a $5,000 limit, staying under $1,500 is healthy and under $500 is ideal.

What Carrying a Balance Actually Costs

Interest rates are abstract until you see them in dollars. According to Federal Reserve G.19 data, the average APR across all credit card accounts was 20.94 percent in the second quarter of 2026. For accounts actually carrying a balance it was 22.15 percent, and the average rate offered on a new card was 23.79 percent.

Here is what that means per month, before you pay down a single dollar of what you owe.

The annual figure is the one that lands. A $5,000 balance held for a year at 22.15 percent costs about $1,108 in interest alone, and that is if the balance never grows. It buys you nothing. Americans collectively held $1.252 trillion in card balances as of the first quarter of 2026, according to the New York Fed.

This is also why rewards chasing collapses under a carried balance. There is no cash back rate on the market that beats 22 percent going the other way. If you are carrying a balance, the highest-return financial move available to you is almost certainly paying it off, not optimising which card earns 3 percent at the supermarket.

Utilization and Your Credit Score

Utilization is the share of your available credit that you are using, and it is one of the largest inputs into a credit score after payment history. If you hold $10,000 in total limits and carry $3,000 across your cards, your utilization is 30 percent.

Two details catch people out. First, utilization is generally measured on the balance your issuer reports on the statement date, not on the due date, so paying in full can still leave a high figure on your report if you spent heavily that month. Paying down before the statement closes fixes that. Second, both per-card and overall utilization are assessed, so one maxed card can drag your score even when your total looks fine.

Below 30 percent is the conventional guidance and below 10 percent is where the best scores sit. Zero is not the target either; a small reported balance shows the account is genuinely in use.

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

Chasing a bonus you cannot reach

A $750 sign-up bonus commonly demands $4,000 of spending within three months. If that is not spending you would have done anyway, engineering it costs more in fees and interest than the bonus returns.

Reading a credit limit as money you have

A $5,000 limit is not $5,000 of savings. It is $5,000 you may borrow at over 20 percent. The shift from treating credit as available funds to treating it as an emergency bridge is what separates people who use cards from people whose cards use them.

Paying only the minimum

Minimum payments are structured to keep the account profitable for as long as possible. On $5,000 at 22 percent, paying only the minimum can stretch past twenty years and cost more in interest than the original debt. Anything above the minimum, even $25, meaningfully shortens that.

Applying for several cards at once

Each application costs 5 to 10 points temporarily. Three inside a month can push you below the threshold for the card you actually wanted, turning a plan into a string of rejections.

Ignoring the cash advance rules

Cash advances usually carry a higher APR than purchases, charge an upfront fee, and start accruing interest immediately with no grace period. Using a card at an ATM is one of the most expensive things you can do with it.

Credit Card Terms Glossary

The vocabulary issuers use is not always the vocabulary customers use. These are the terms most likely to cause an expensive misunderstanding.

Statement balance

What you owed on the day your billing cycle closed. Paying this in full by the due date is what keeps you interest-free.

Outstanding balance

Everything you currently owe, including purchases made since the statement closed. Usually higher than the statement balance.

Grace period

The window between the statement closing and the due date during which purchases accrue no interest. It applies to purchases only, and you lose it while carrying a balance.

Utilization ratio

Balance divided by credit limit, measured per card and overall. One of the strongest score factors you can change quickly.

CID, CVV and CVC

The short security code proving you hold the card. American Express calls it the CID and prints four digits on the front; others use three digits on the back.

Cash advance

Withdrawing cash against the card. Higher APR, an upfront fee, and interest from day one with no grace period.

Hard and soft inquiry

A hard inquiry follows an application and affects your score. A soft inquiry, such as checking your own report, does not.

Adverse action notice

The explanation an issuer must send when it declines you. It names the specific reason, which makes it the most useful document you can receive after a rejection.

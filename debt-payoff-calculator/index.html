How to Use This Calculator

Follow these steps to plan your debt-free date and see how extra payments save you money.

  1. Enter your total debt balanceThe full amount you currently owe across all accounts you want to pay off together. For credit cards, use the statement balance, not the minimum payment.
  2. Add your interest rate (APR)Found on your statement or online banking. Credit cards typically run 18-29% APR. Personal loans range 7-20%. Auto loans 5-12%. Mortgages 6-7% in 2026.
  3. Set your monthly paymentWhat you can realistically commit to paying each month. Must be higher than your monthly interest charge, or you'll never pay it off.
  4. (Optional) Add extra monthly paymentSee how much faster you'd be debt-free if you paid an extra $50, $100, or $200 per month. The savings are usually shocking — try different amounts.
  5. (Optional) Add a one-time lump sumGot a tax refund, bonus, or unexpected money? Enter it here and pick a month to apply it. Watch how much interest you save.
  6. Review the chart4 lines show you exactly where each dollar goes: balance declining (red), money out (purple), principal paid (green), interest lost (gold). The gap between green and gold tells the story.
  7. Use the year-by-year tableSee your progress for each year of payoff — exactly when you cross milestones.

⚠️ Important Disclaimer

Results are estimates only. The values shown by this calculator are mathematical projections based on the inputs you provide. Actual payoff timelines may vary if your interest rate changes (common with variable-rate credit cards), if you miss payments and incur fees, or if you take on additional debt during the payoff period.

Always verify your exact balance, APR, and minimum payment with your lender or credit card company before making decisions. Promotional 0% APR periods can change to high rates suddenly. Check your statement carefully.

How to Pay Off Debt Faster

The debt payoff calculator shows you exactly when you'll be debt-free based on your current balance, interest rate, and monthly payment. Even small increases in your monthly payment can dramatically reduce your payoff timeline and total interest paid.

Debt Snowball vs Debt Avalanche

The snowball method pays smallest balances first for quick psychological wins. The avalanche method targets highest-interest debt first to minimize total interest. Both work the best method is the one you stick with consistently. If you have multiple debts, use this calculator for each one separately.

The True Cost of Minimum Payments

Paying only the minimum on a $5,000 credit card at 22% APR can take over 20 years and cost more than $8,000 in interest alone. Doubling your payment cuts the timeline to under 3 years and saves thousands. Use the "extra payment" feature above to see for yourself.

Balance Transfer Cards

If your debt is on a high-APR credit card, a 0% APR balance transfer card can save thousands in interest. These cards typically offer 0% for 12-21 months on transferred balances. Compare options on our credit cards page. There's usually a 3-5% transfer fee, but the interest savings often dwarf the fee.

When to Consider Debt Consolidation

Consolidating multiple high-interest debts into one personal loan with a lower rate can simplify payments and save money. This works best when you can get a lower interest rate AND you commit not to run up new credit card balances after consolidating.

Building an Emergency Fund First

Before going all-in on debt payoff, save at least $1,000 in an emergency fund. Without one, an unexpected car repair or medical bill goes straight onto your credit cards, undoing your progress.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take to pay off $10,000 in credit card debt?

At 22% APR with $300 monthly payments, it takes about 4 years and costs roughly $4,400 in interest. Increasing to $500/month cuts it to about 2 years and saves over $2,000 in interest. Use this calculator with your exact numbers to find your debt-free date.

Is it better to save or pay off debt first?

Save a small emergency fund ($1,000) first, then aggressively pay off high-interest debt (anything above 7% APR), then build a full 3-6 month emergency fund. Credit card debt at 20%+ APR almost always beats saving you'd need investments to earn 20%+ to come out ahead, which is unrealistic.

Should I pay off debt with my savings?

If your savings earn less interest than your debt charges (almost always true with credit cards), then yes — use savings beyond your emergency fund to pay down debt. But never empty your emergency fund completely. Keep at least $1,000 untouched.

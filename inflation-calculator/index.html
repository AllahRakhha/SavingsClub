How This Inflation Calculator Works

Inflation is simply the rate at which prices rise, which means the same dollar buys a little less each year. To measure it, the Bureau of Labor Statistics tracks the price of a fixed basket of goods and services and publishes the result as the Consumer Price Index for All Urban Consumers, usually shortened to CPI-U. The index is set so that the 1982 to 1984 period equals 100.

Once you have index values, converting money between two years is straightforward. This calculator holds the annual average CPI for every year from 1913 onward and applies one formula:

Value in target year equals the original amount multiplied by the target year CPI divided by the starting year CPI.
Amount x (CPI target / CPI start)

That is the same arithmetic the Federal Reserve and the BLS use, which is why a proper CPI-based result differs from the rough "3 percent a year" estimate most simple tools give you. Real inflation was never a smooth 3 percent. It was 17.4 percent in 1917, negative 10.5 percent in 1932, 13.5 percent in 1980, and 8.0 percent in 2022.

The forward mode works differently on purpose. Nobody knows future CPI, so that side of the calculator compounds a rate you choose and shows both what a future purchase will cost and what today's money will feel like once prices have moved.

How to Read Your Results

The calculator returns more than one number, and each answers a different question.

The headline figure

In past-to-today mode, this is your original amount restated in the target year's money. It answers "what would I need now to buy what that bought then." In future mode, it is what a purchase costing your amount today would cost after inflation.

Total inflation and the annual average

Total inflation is the full percentage change in prices across the whole span. The annual average is the compound yearly rate that would produce the same result, which is almost always lower than people expect. A 900 percent total increase over 75 years works out to only about 3.1 percent a year, because compounding does the heavy lifting.

Purchasing power lost

In forward mode this shows the dollar amount of buying power that evaporates over your chosen period. It is the number worth showing to anyone who keeps large balances in a near-zero interest account, because it makes an invisible loss visible.

The chart

The line traces the value of your money year by year. In past mode it climbs, showing how many of today's dollars it takes to match the original sum. In future mode it falls, showing today's money shrinking in real terms.

How to Calculate the Inflation Rate Yourself

You do not need software for this. The standard method uses two CPI values.

Inflation rate equals CPI now minus CPI then, divided by CPI then, multiplied by 100.
((CPI_new - CPI_old) / CPI_old) x 100

A worked example: the CPI annual average was 240.0 in 2016 and 321.9 in 2025. Subtracting gives 81.9, dividing by 240.0 gives 0.341, and multiplying by 100 gives about 34.1 percent total inflation across those nine years.

Turning a total into an annual rate

To find the average annual rate rather than the total, take the ratio of the two index values, raise it to the power of one divided by the number of years, and subtract one. For the example above that is 34.1 percent total, but roughly 3.3 percent a year.

How to calculate inflation in Excel

Spreadsheets make this quick. Put the older CPI value in cell A1 and the newer one in B1, then:

  • Total inflation: =(B1-A1)/A1 formatted as a percentage
  • Convert an amount in C1: =C1*(B1/A1)
  • Average annual rate across n years: =(B1/A1)^(1/n)-1

The same three formulas work in Google Sheets without modification. If you want to see how those rates compound over time on the savings side instead, our compound interest calculator runs the reverse of this arithmetic.

The years worth knowing

The single worst year on record was 1917 at 17.4 percent. The sharpest fall was 1932 at negative 10.5 percent, in the depths of the Depression. The modern peak was 1980 at 13.5 percent, which is why anyone who bought a house that year remembers the mortgage rate. Most recently, 2022 hit 8.0 percent, the highest in four decades, before easing back toward the Fed's target.

The lesson from the table is not that inflation averages 3 percent. It is that inflation arrives in bursts, and the bursts are what damage a financial plan built on a flat assumption.

Inflation and Retirement Planning

Inflation does more damage to retirement plans than to any other part of personal finance, because retirement is the longest horizon most people plan for and the one where income is least flexible.

The arithmetic is unforgiving. If you need $50,000 a year to live on today and prices rise at 3 percent, you need roughly $67,000 in year ten and about $90,000 by year twenty to fund the identical lifestyle. A thirty year retirement can require your income to more than double along the way.

How long will savings actually last

Any projection that holds withdrawals flat in nominal dollars will overstate how long your money lasts, sometimes by many years. When you model retirement, raise your annual withdrawal by your assumed inflation rate each year. Our retirement calculator and 401(k) calculator are built to account for this.

What about Social Security

Social Security benefits do receive an annual cost-of-living adjustment tied to a version of the CPI, which helps considerably. The catch is that the adjustment is backward looking, arriving after prices have already risen, and the basket it tracks does not weight medical costs the way most retirees experience them. Treat it as partial protection rather than full protection.

What Is an Inflation Premium?

An inflation premium is the extra return investors require to compensate them for expected price rises. Nobody willingly lends money at 3 percent if they expect prices to climb 5 percent, because they would lose purchasing power on the deal.

Rough form: nominal interest rate minus real interest rate equals the inflation premium.
Inflation premium = Nominal rate - Real rate

How to calculate inflation premium in practice

The cleanest market estimate is the breakeven inflation rate. Take the yield on a regular Treasury bond and subtract the yield on a Treasury Inflation-Protected Security of the same maturity. The gap is roughly what the market expects average inflation to be over that period. If the ten year Treasury yields 4.3 percent and the ten year TIPS yields 2.0 percent, the implied premium is about 2.3 percent.

This matters beyond bond markets. It is the reason savings account rates rise when inflation rises, and the reason a certificate of deposit locked in before an inflation spike can end up losing real value even while paying interest. Our CD calculator shows nominal returns, so compare the result against the inflation rate to see the real one.

How to Protect Your Money From Inflation

You cannot stop inflation, but you can hold assets that tend to outrun it.

  • Broad stock index funds. Historically the strongest long-run hedge, though with real volatility along the way. Companies can raise prices, and earnings tend to rise with the price level.
  • Series I savings bonds. The rate is tied directly to the CPI, so the return adjusts as inflation moves. Purchase limits apply per person per year.
  • Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities. The principal itself adjusts with the CPI, which makes them the most literal protection available.
  • Real estate. Property values and rents have generally tracked or exceeded inflation over long periods, though location matters enormously.
  • High-yield savings. Not a growth strategy, but for the cash you genuinely need liquid, a rate above current inflation stops the erosion. Compare rates with our savings calculator.

The mistake to avoid is the quiet one: leaving a large balance in a checking account paying almost nothing. At 3 percent inflation, $50,000 sitting idle loses about $1,500 of purchasing power a year, every year, without a single transaction appearing on the statement.

What the CPI Does Not Capture

Every inflation calculator, including this one, inherits the limitations of the index behind it. Being honest about them makes the results more useful, not less.

Your basket is not the average basket. The CPI weights a nationwide urban household's spending. If you rent in an expensive city, your personal inflation rate over the last few years was almost certainly higher than the headline. If you own with a fixed mortgage, it was probably lower.

Quality changes are adjusted for. The BLS adjusts prices when products improve, so a car that costs more but is safer and lasts longer may not register as full inflation. Reasonable people disagree about whether these adjustments are too generous.

Very long comparisons stretch the concept. Converting an 1913 amount into 2026 dollars is arithmetically valid, but the basket of goods has changed so completely that the comparison is directional rather than precise. Nobody in 1913 was buying broadband.

Assets are not in the index. House prices and stock prices are not CPI components, so periods of rapid asset inflation can feel far more expensive than the official rate suggests.

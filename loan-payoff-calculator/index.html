How this loan payoff calculator works

Enter three numbers: your current balance, your interest rate, and your monthly payment. The calculator runs your loan month by month, charging interest on the remaining balance and applying your payment, exactly the way your lender does. That gives you your real payoff date and total interest cost, not an approximation.

Then it runs the loan a second time with your extra monthly payment and any one-time lump sum included, and puts the two side by side. The difference between those two runs is the honest answer to the question everyone actually has: what does paying extra really buy me? The answer is measured in months removed from the loan and dollars of interest that never get charged.

This works as a personal loan payoff calculator, an auto loan payoff calculator, or a payoff calculator for any fixed-rate installment loan. Why extra payments are so powerful

Every dollar of an extra payment goes to principal (when applied correctly, more on that below). Shrinking the principal does two things at once: it directly reduces what you owe, and it reduces the balance that next month's interest is calculated on. That second effect compounds in your favor every single month for the rest of the loan.

The numbers are bigger than intuition suggests. On a $25,000 loan at 9% with a $500 monthly payment, you would pay the loan off in about 63 months with roughly $6,400 in total interest. Add just $100 a month and the loan is gone about a year sooner, with around $1,400 less interest paid. Add $250 a month and you cut the loan by nearly two years. Run your own numbers above; the calculator shows the exact figures for your loan.

A one-time lump sum works the same way, and earlier is dramatically better than later. A $2,000 lump sum in month one saves far more interest than the same $2,000 in year three, because it spends more months reducing the balance that interest is charged on.

The biweekly payment trick, explained honestly

You will see biweekly payments promoted as a magic loan hack. Here is what it actually is: paying half your monthly payment every two weeks produces 26 half payments per year, which equals 13 full payments instead of 12. It is simply one extra monthly payment per year, automated.

That extra payment is genuinely useful, and the automation is the real benefit, since no willpower or monthly decision is required. But you can get the identical result by making one extra principal payment yourself each year, free. If your lender or a third party charges setup or per-payment fees for a biweekly program, skip the program and do it manually. Never pay a fee for arithmetic.

Pay off the loan early or invest the difference?

This is the real decision behind every early payoff plan, and the honest answer depends on your interest rate. Paying extra on a loan is a guaranteed return equal to your interest rate. Paying down a 9% loan is a guaranteed 9%, which beats what most diversified investments can promise. Paying down a 3% loan early, while skipping investments that have historically averaged 6 to 7% after inflation, usually leaves money on the table over long periods.

The rough framework most financial educators use: high-rate debt (above about 7 to 8%) is worth attacking aggressively; low-rate debt (below about 4 to 5%) is usually worth paying on schedule while investing the difference; the middle is a judgment call where guaranteed savings, risk tolerance, and how much you value being debt free all matter. Freeing up a monthly payment also reduces your risk if your income drops, which is worth something no spreadsheet captures. Compare the investing side with our Investment Calculator and make the call with real numbers on both sides.

One order-of-operations rule almost everyone agrees on: build a basic emergency fund before making big extra loan payments. Extra payments are usually irreversible; you cannot ask the lender for the money back when the transmission dies.

Common early payoff mistakes

Not telling the lender to apply extra money to principal. This is the expensive one. Many lenders apply extra money to your next scheduled payment by default, which prepays interest instead of reducing your balance. When you pay extra, select "apply to principal" in the app or say those exact words on the phone, then verify on your next statement that the balance dropped by the full extra amount.

Ignoring prepayment penalties. Most personal and auto loans today have none, but some do, especially older loans and certain subprime auto loans. Check your loan agreement for the words prepayment penalty or precomputed interest before making large extra payments. A precomputed loan (interest calculated up front) makes early payoff far less valuable.

Paying extra on a 6% loan while carrying a 24% credit card. Interest rate order matters. Extra dollars belong on the most expensive debt first. Our Debt Payoff Calculator orders your whole debt list for you.

Draining every dollar into the loan. Being debt free with zero savings is fragile. Keep the emergency fund funded, capture any 401(k) employer match first (that is an instant 50 to 100% return, better than any loan rate), then attack the loan with what remains.

People Often Ask

How do I calculate my loan payoff early?

Enter your current balance, interest rate, and monthly payment above, then add the extra amount you can pay each month or as a one-time lump sum. The calculator runs your loan month by month and shows the new payoff date, the months you save, and the interest you avoid compared with your current plan.

Do extra payments automatically go to the loan principal?

Not always, and this mistake costs people real money. Some lenders apply extra money to your next scheduled payment (interest included) instead of reducing principal. When you make an extra payment, mark it as principal-only in your lender's app, or call and say the words apply to principal. Then check the next statement to confirm the balance dropped by the full amount.

How much time does an extra $100 a month actually save?

It depends on your balance and rate, but the effect is bigger than most people expect. On a $25,000 loan at 9% with a $500 payment, adding $100 a month pays the loan off about a year sooner and saves roughly $1,400 in interest. Run your own numbers above to see the exact impact.

Are biweekly payments better than one extra monthly payment?

They are almost the same thing in disguise. Paying half your monthly payment every two weeks results in 26 half-payments per year, which equals 13 full payments instead of 12. The advantage of biweekly is psychological: the extra payment happens automatically without a decision every month. If your lender charges fees for biweekly programs, skip the program and just make one extra principal payment per year yourself.

Should I pay off my loan early or invest the extra money?

Compare your loan's interest rate to what you could reasonably earn elsewhere. Paying off a 9% loan is a guaranteed 9% return, which beats most realistic investment expectations. Paying off a 3% loan early while skipping investments that could earn 6 to 7% usually costs you money over time. Also weigh the non-math factors: a paid-off loan frees up monthly cash flow and reduces risk if your income drops.

Does paying off a loan early hurt your credit score?

It can cause a small, temporary dip. Closing an installment account can reduce your credit mix and lower the average age of your accounts. For most people the dip is minor and recovers within months, and the interest savings are worth far more than a few temporary points. Do not keep a loan open just to feed your credit score.

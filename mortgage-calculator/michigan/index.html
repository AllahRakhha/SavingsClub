Understanding mortgages in Michigan

Michigan offers some of the most affordable home prices among the top 10 most populous states. The combination of moderate prices ($245,000 median, 42% below the national median of $420,000), Michigan's flat 4.25% state income tax, strong Great Lakes lifestyle, and the Headlee Amendment's property tax protections has made Michigan increasingly attractive — both for first-time buyers and remote workers leaving expensive coastal markets. Detroit's downtown revitalization, Grand Rapids' steady growth, and Ann Arbor's biotech and university strength have created genuine opportunity. Check your real take-home pay in Michigan if you're comparing to other states.

The current 30-year fixed mortgage rate in Michigan averages approximately 6.46%, slightly below the national average. The state has a competitive lending market regional credit unions (Lake Michigan Credit Union, Michigan First, Genisys) often beat national lender rates by 0.10–0.25% for well qualified buyers.

The Headlee Amendment: Michigan's most important tax law for buyers

Michigan's Headlee Amendment (1978) combined with Proposal A (1994) caps annual increases in your taxable property value at 5% or the rate of inflation, whichever is lower as long as you own the home. This protects long term homeowners from skyrocketing tax bills during real estate booms. But there's a critical catch for buyers: when you sell, the property is "uncapped" and reassessed at full market value. New buyers can face dramatically higher tax bills than the previous owner paid.

Practical impact: when shopping for a Michigan home, ALWAYS ask for the seller's most recent tax bill AND the property's State Equalized Value (SEV). Your taxes will be based on the SEV often 30–50% higher than what the previous owner was paying. Many new Michigan buyers experience sticker shock at their first tax bill because they only looked at last year's number. Use our budget planner to plan around the post-uncapping tax, not the seller's number.

The Principal Residence Exemption (PRE) — Michigan's most overlooked savings

Michigan's Principal Residence Exemption (PRE) eliminates 18 mills of school operating tax on your primary residence — typically saving $700–$1,500 per year depending on your property's taxable value. It is NOT automatic. Many new buyers forget to file in the chaos of closing.

💰 Filing your PRE — what you need to know
File Form 2368 within 60 days of closing. The Principal Residence Exemption Affidavit goes to your COUNTY treasurer (or local assessor in some jurisdictions) within 60 days of becoming the owner. Miss the deadline and you pay the full school operating mills for the year. Set a calendar reminder for week 6 after closing.
Savings: ~$700–$1,500/year for typical Michigan homes. The PRE removes 18 mills of school operating tax. On a $245K home with $122,500 taxable value, that's roughly $2,200/year but the simplified estimate the calculator uses is $900/year (conservative across counties). The actual savings depend on your specific local school district millage.
Only applies to your PRIMARY residence not rentals, vacation homes, or second homes. If you have multiple Michigan properties (like a Traverse City cottage and a Royal Oak primary), only one gets the PRE. Use our net worth calculator to track equity across multiple properties.
You can also claim the federal Mortgage Credit Certificate (MCC) for additional savings. The MCC is a federal tax credit worth 20% of mortgage interest paid (up to $2,000/year). Available to first time MI buyers through MSHDA. Combines with the PRE for stacked savings. Run your 401(k) calculator with the after-tax savings.

The Detroit Land Bank Authority: real opportunity, real caveats

Unlike any other US city, Detroit has the Detroit Land Bank Authority (DLBA) a public entity that owns and sells thousands of abandoned and city owned residential properties at extreme low prices. Auction starting bids run as low as $1,000–$10,000. For the right buyer (with renovation skills or budget), this is a real path to homeownership unavailable anywhere else in America. For most buyers, it's a trap.

🏚️ The Detroit Land Bank — opportunity with serious caveats
Starting bids: $1,000 to $10,000. Most DLBA properties auction at the low end. Eligible neighborhoods include both stabilizing areas (potential upside) and areas with significant vacancy and infrastructure challenges. Check buildingdetroit.org for current listings.
Mandatory renovation requirement: typically 6 months to 1 year. You CAN'T just buy and hold. The DLBA requires substantial rehabilitation within a defined timeline. Miss the deadline and the property reverts to the Land Bank. This protects neighborhoods from speculative purchase and hold but creates real pressure on buyers.
Renovation cost: typically $50,000–$200,000+. Most DLBA properties need complete systems work (plumbing, electrical, HVAC, roof, foundation), plus interior rehab. Get a contractor inspection before bidding. Don't underestimate this — undercapitalized buyers regularly lose their DLBA properties.
Who this works for: Skilled buyers with contractor experience, $100K+ renovation budget, or buyers willing to live in temporary housing during the rebuild. Who this does NOT work for: First time buyers with no renovation experience, anyone with thin cash reserves, or anyone unable to move quickly when issues arise.
Bottom line: A genuine wealth building opportunity for the right buyer profile, but the renovation reality means the "$1,000 home" usually costs $150,000+ all in by the time you're moving in. Run the numbers carefully with our savings planner and debt payoff calculator before bidding.

Michigan first-time homebuyer programs (MSHDA)

The Michigan State Housing Development Authority (MSHDA) offers several genuinely useful programs. Use our retirement calculator to factor lifetime tax savings into your big picture planning.

MSHDA MI Home Loan

Competitive 30 year fixed rate mortgage for first time buyers (or repeat buyers in targeted areas). Often 0.25–0.50% below market rates for income qualified buyers. Available through MSHDA approved lenders statewide.

MI 10K DPA Loan

Up to $10,000 down payment assistance as a 0% interest second mortgage. NO monthly payments repayment deferred until you sell, refinance, or pay off the first mortgage. Must be combined with MSHDA MI Home Loan.

Mortgage Credit Certificate (MCC)

A federal tax credit worth 20% of annual mortgage interest paid (up to $2,000 per year, every year you have the loan). Reduces your federal income tax bill directly not just a deduction. Available to first time buyers and certain veterans through MSHDA.

MI Home Loan Flex

MSHDA's option for repeat buyers and higher income borrowers who don't qualify for the standard MI Home Loan. Still offers competitive rates with optional DPA. Income limits are higher; first time buyer status not required.

Frequently Asked Questions
What is the average mortgage rate in Michigan in 2026?
As of June 2026, Michigan 30 year fixed mortgage rates average approximately 6.46% slightly below the national average. Local credit unions like Lake Michigan Credit Union, Michigan First, and Genisys often offer competitive rates 0.10–0.25% below national lenders for well qualified buyers. Borrowers with credit scores above 740 typically see additional rate reductions of 0.25–0.5%.
What is the Headlee Amendment and how does it affect new buyers?
Michigan's Headlee Amendment (1978) combined with Proposal A (1994) caps annual increases in your taxable property value at 5% or the rate of inflation (whichever is less) as long as you own the home. BUT when you BUY a Michigan home, the property "uncaps" and reassesses at full market value — meaning your first year's tax bill is often 30–50% HIGHER than what the seller was paying. Always check the State Equalized Value (SEV) in the listing not just last year's tax bill before making an offer.
What is the Principal Residence Exemption (PRE) and how do I get it?
Michigan's PRE eliminates 18 mills of school operating tax on your primary residence typically saving $700–$1,500/year. File Form 2368 (the PRE Affidavit) with your county treasurer within 60 days of closing. It's NOT automatic. The PRE only applies to your primary residence; vacation homes and rentals don't qualify. Why are Detroit home prices so low?
Detroit's citywide median of $95,000 reflects the city's population decline (over 60% from its 1950 peak), aging housing stock, and historical service challenges. But this median is MISLEADING it averages habitable rehabbed homes with thousands of abandoned and blighted properties that aren't realistic purchase targets. Revitalized neighborhoods like Corktown, Midtown, Indian Village, Boston Edison, and downtown regularly sell for $300,000–$600,000. Research specific neighborhoods carefully and budget for renovation if you go below $200K.
Should I buy in Detroit, Grand Rapids, or Ann Arbor?
Different markets for different buyers. Detroit ($95K citywide / $300K+ in revitalized neighborhoods) offers extreme affordability but requires careful neighborhood research and often renovation. Grand Rapids ($305K) has strong healthcare, brewing, and tech jobs with family friendly suburbs. Ann Arbor ($465K) has the University of Michigan, biotech, and top rated schools at coastal state prices. Match your budget AND your industry to the right city.

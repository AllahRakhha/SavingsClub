Understanding mortgages in New York

New York has one of the most complex real estate markets in America. The state combines extreme regional variation (Manhattan median exceeds $1.15M while Buffalo is under $215K), unique buyer-paid taxes that don't exist in other states, and one of the highest state income taxes in the country. New York real estate transactions can include closing costs of 4–6% of purchase price for the buyer — significantly higher than the 2–3% typical elsewhere.

The current 30 year fixed mortgage rate in New York averages approximately 6.51%, very close to the national average. Most national lenders compete heavily in NY, but the unique market structure — including the prevalence of co-op ownership in NYC — adds complexity that doesn't exist in other states.

NYC mansion tax, mortgage recording tax, and transfer tax

New York City buyers face several extra taxes at closing that often surprise out-of-state buyers. These are the most distinctive feature of the NY market and can add five to seven percent to your buyer-side closing costs:

NYC Mansion Tax (buyer pays) Additional tax on residential purchases of $1M or more. 1% on $1M–$1.99M, scaling up to 3.9% on purchases over $25M. The tax applies to the full purchase price, not just the amount over $1M. A $1.5M Brooklyn townhouse triggers $15,000 in mansion tax.
Mortgage Recording Tax (NYC) 1.8% on loans under $500K, 2.8% on loans of $500K or more, within NYC. A $700,000 mortgage triggers approximately $19,600 in recording tax paid at closing. Some lenders offer to absorb a portion of this — always ask.
Transfer Tax (NY State + NYC) NY State charges a 0.4% transfer tax. NYC adds 1% on properties up to $500K and 1.425% above. Usually paid by the seller but sometimes negotiable in slow markets.
Attorney fees (required in NY) New York is one of the few states that requires a real estate attorney at closing typically $1,500–$3,000 per side. Most other states use title companies, not attorneys, so out of state buyers often forget to budget for this.

New York property tax: extreme variation by county

New York's statewide property tax average of 1.40% tells you almost nothing about what you'll actually pay. Effective rates range from under 0.85% (NYC, due to assessment caps) to over 2.95% in some Western NY counties. For a median $475,000 home statewide, you'd pay approximately $6,650/year but Westchester or Long Island owners commonly pay $15,000–$25,000/year on similar sized homes.

That's roughly the same dollar amount a much more expensive NYC home pays, despite costing a fraction of the price.

Median home prices across New York

Statewide median is approximately $475,000, but New York is really at least five different housing markets stacked into one state: Manhattan, the outer boroughs, the NYC suburbs (Westchester, Long Island), the Hudson Valley, and Western/Upstate NY. Prices in each can differ by 5–7x.

New York first-time homebuyer programs

New York offers solid first-time buyer assistance through the State of New York Mortgage Agency (SONYMA) and various NYC specific programs. Income and purchase price limits vary by county limits in high cost downstate counties are noticeably higher than upstate.

SONYMA (State of New York Mortgage Agency)

Competitive 30-year fixed-rate mortgages with down payment assistance up to $15,000 or 3% of the home price. Income and purchase price limits apply by county. SONYMA loans are originated through participating lenders statewide.

NYC HomeFirst Down Payment Assistance

Up to $100,000 in forgivable down payment and closing cost assistance for low to moderate income first time buyers purchasing in NYC. The loan is forgiven over 10 to 15 years if you stay in the home. Highly competitive applications open in limited windows.

First-Time Homebuyer Credit (NYC)

NYC offers a partial Mortgage Recording Tax credit for first time buyers on loans under $200,000 saves approximately $3,000–$5,000 at closing. Income limits and other eligibility requirements apply.

SONYMA Achieving the Dream

A specialized program offering below market interest rates plus down payment assistance for lower income buyers. Combines with SONYMA's standard programs to layer multiple forms of help.

SavingsClub Tips for New York Buyers
Three New York specific things most out-of-state buyers don't know
1
Budget 5–6% for buyer side closing costs, not the 2–3% national rule of thumb. Between Mortgage Recording Tax (NYC), mansion tax (if $1M+), transfer tax, required attorney fees ($1,500–$3,000), and title insurance, New York has some of the highest closing costs in America. On a $700,000 NYC home, that's roughly $35,000–$42,000 in closing costs — separate from your down payment.
2
The seller's property tax bill is not your property tax bill. NYC has STAR (School Tax Relief) credits and senior exemptions that may be tied to the current owner and disappear when ownership changes. Westchester and Long Island properties may have local school district homestead exemptions that also reset. Always calculate your tax estimate from the sale price multiplied by your county's effective rate, not from the listing's disclosed bill.
3
If you're buying a co-op (mostly NYC), the financing rules are completely different. Co-op boards typically require 20–30% down, post-closing reserves equal to 1–2 years of maintenance, and full board approval (which can take 2–3 months and includes rejecting buyers without explanation). Most national mortgage lender programs don't apply to co-op loans. Get a co-op-experienced lender lined up before you make an offer most national online lenders won't touch them.
Frequently Asked Questions
What is the average mortgage rate in New York in 2026?
As of June 2026, New York 30 year fixed mortgage rates average approximately 6.51%, very close to the national average. Co-op loans (specific to NYC) often have slightly different rates and require board approval in addition to standard lender underwriting.
What is the NYC mansion tax?
The NYC mansion tax is an additional buyer paid tax on residential purchases of $1M or more. It scales from 1% (at $1M) up to 3.9% (over $25M). On a $1.5M Brooklyn home, you pay $15,000 in mansion tax at closing on top of other taxes. The tax applies to the full purchase price, not just to the amount over $1M.
What is the New York mortgage recording tax?
The mortgage recording tax is a tax on the loan amount (not the purchase price), paid at closing. In NYC, the rate is 1.8% on loans under $500,000 and 2.8% on loans of $500,000 or more. On a $700,000 NYC mortgage, that's approximately $19,600 — separate from transfer tax, mansion tax, and other closing costs. Outside NYC, the rate is lower but still applies.
Why does property tax vary so much across New York?
Local school districts and municipalities set their own rates. Wealthy suburbs with excellent schools (Westchester, Long Island) levy 2.0%–2.4% effective rates. NYC pays a lower 0.85% but with severe assessment caps that distort values. Western New York (Buffalo, Rochester) pays the highest rates (2.5–3%) but on much cheaper homes. The statewide average of 1.40% is the math but it doesn't describe what most homeowners actually pay.
Should I buy upstate or downstate?
Upstate (Buffalo, Rochester, Albany, Syracuse) offers some of the most affordable housing in America — median prices under $300K. Property tax rates are higher (2.5–3%) but on cheaper homes. Downstate offers strong job markets and appreciation potential but at much higher entry prices. Match your decision to job location, lifestyle, and how much annual property tax you can carry.
What closing costs should I expect in New York?
NY closing costs are among the highest in the U.S.typically 4 to 6% of purchase price for buyers (vs 2–3% national average). This includes Mortgage Recording Tax, mansion tax (if applicable), attorney fees ($1,500–$3,000 NY is one of the few states that requires attorneys at closing), title insurance, transfer taxes, and various smaller fees. Budget more for closing in NY than in any other state besides possibly Hawaii.

Understanding mortgages in Ohio

Ohio is the most affordable of America's top 10 most populous states for homebuyers, with a median home price of approximately $235,000 about 44% below the national median of $420,000. The state combines low entry prices with moderate property tax (1.36% effective) and a graduated state income tax that maxes out at 3.5%. But Ohio has a hidden tax layer most out-of-state buyers don't expect most Ohio cities also charge their own income tax of 1–2.5%. Check your real take-home pay after both state AND city tax before committing.

The current 30-year fixed mortgage rate in Ohio averages approximately 6.47%, slightly below the national average. Ohio has a strong network of community banks and credit unions Telhio, Wright-Patt, KEMBA, Dover-Phila that often offer rates 0.10–0.20% below national lenders for well-qualified buyers, especially in smaller cities.

Ohio's hidden tax layer: city income tax (RITA / CCA)

Ohio is one of the only states in America where most cities charge their own income tax on top of state tax. Over 330 Ohio municipalities have their own income tax, administered through RITA (Regional Income Tax Agency) or CCA (Central Collection Agency). You owe city tax even if you LIVE outside the city but WORK there. This is the most underestimated cost for out-of-state buyers moving to Ohio.

⚠️ Ohio city income tax — the hidden cost
Columbus, Cleveland, Toledo, Akron, Dayton: 2.5% city income tax. For $100K income, that's $2,500/year IN ADDITION to Ohio state tax (up to 3.5%).
Cincinnati: 1.8% city income tax — lower than other major Ohio cities but still meaningful at $1,800/year on $100K income.
The "live in suburb, work in city" trap: If you live in Westlake (Cleveland suburb) but work in Cleveland, you owe Cleveland's 2.5% — your suburb gives partial credit but rarely full. Same dynamic for Columbus, Cincinnati, and Toledo metros. ALWAYS verify your work location's city tax before committing.
Combined state + city + property tax burden in Cleveland or Columbus: A $100K income with a $250K home faces approximately 3% state + 2.5% city = 5.5% income tax PLUS property tax. Compare to Florida (0% state income tax) or Texas (0% state income tax) when running total cost of living.

Ohio property tax by county

Ohio's effective property tax rate of 1.36% is moderately above the national average. For a median $235,000 Ohio home, that's approximately $3,196 per year, or $266 per month. But variation between counties is dramatic — Cuyahoga County's effective rate (2.13%) is nearly double Franklin County's (1.55%).

Ohio's two homeowner exemptions modest but free money

Ohio has two property tax exemptions worth filing for neither is automatic, both require an application through your county auditor.

💰 Ohio Owner-Occupancy Credit + Homestead Exemption
Owner-Occupancy Credit (OOC) — 2.5% reduction. Provides a 2.5% reduction in voted property taxes for primary residences. Modest savings typically $60–$100 per year on a median Ohio home. Free to file. Apply through your county auditor most counties allow online filing. Not automatic.
Homestead Exemption (65+ / disabled) $25,000 reduction in taxable value. For homeowners 65+ or permanently disabled with household income under approximately $38,600 (2026 limit). Typically saves $400–$700 per year. There's also a separate Homestead Exemption for disabled veterans with NO income limit. Apply between January 1 and December 31 with your county auditor.
These stack: A qualifying senior homeowner can claim BOTH the OOC (2.5% reduction) AND the Homestead Exemption ($25K taxable value reduction) for combined savings of $500–$800/year. Run lifetime savings through our retirement calculator if planning to age in place in Ohio.
Disabled Veteran Full Exemption: Veterans rated 100% disabled by the VA qualify for a full property tax exemption on their primary residence saves the entire property tax bill. File annually with your county auditor.

Columbus is one of the fastest-growing US metros Intel's announced multibillion-dollar semiconductor investment in Licking County, Ohio State University expansion, strong healthcare and insurance employers (Nationwide, OhioHealth, JPMorgan Chase), and steady tech company relocations. Population is projected to add 700,000+ residents through 2050. Home values have grown 8–12% annually in recent years currently Ohio's best market for long-term appreciation. Ohio first time homebuyer programs (OHFA)

The Ohio Housing Finance Agency (OHFA) offers solid first-time buyer programs. Use our 401(k) calculator to balance saving for the down payment against retirement contributions.

OHFA YourChoice! Down Payment Assistance

Choice of 2.5% or 5% of the home's purchase price toward down payment and closing costs. Provided as a 0% interest, forgivable second mortgage with no monthly payment forgiven after 7 years if you remain in the home. Combines with OHFA 30-year fixed-rate mortgage. Income and purchase price limits by county.

Grants for Grads

Recent college graduates (within 48 months) get 2.5% or 5% down payment assistance PLUS a discounted mortgage rate. Available to anyone with an associate, bachelor, master, or professional degree. Stacks well with YourChoice DPA.

Ohio Heroes

Discounted mortgage rates (typically 0.25% below market) for full time teachers, firefighters, EMTs, peace officers, healthcare workers, and active military service members. Can combine with down payment assistance programs.

Frequently Asked Questions
What is the average mortgage rate in Ohio in 2026?
As of June 2026, Ohio 30 year fixed mortgage rates average approximately 6.47% slightly below the national average of 6.53%. Local Ohio credit unions (Telhio, Wright-Patt, KEMBA, Dover-Phila) often offer rates 0.10–0.20% below national lenders for well-qualified buyers, especially in smaller cities. Borrowers with credit scores above 740 typically see additional rate reductions of 0.25–0.5%.
Does Ohio have city income tax?
Yes — Ohio is unusual in this regard. Over 330 Ohio municipalities have their own income tax (1.0–2.5%) on top of state tax. Columbus, Cleveland, Toledo, Akron, Dayton charge 2.5%; Cincinnati charges 1.8%. Critically: you owe city tax even if you live OUTSIDE the city but WORK there. Administered through RITA (Regional Income Tax Agency) or CCA. Always factor city tax into your total cost of living when comparing Ohio to no-state-income tax states like Florida or Texas.
Why is Cleveland so much cheaper than Columbus?
Different economic trajectories. Columbus has strong tech, healthcare, university job growth (including Intel's announced multibillion-dollar Licking County semiconductor investment), and home values have grown 8–12% annually. Cleveland has had decades of population loss, slower job growth, and aging housing stock. Cleveland's citywide median of $135K masks neighborhood variation Tremont, Ohio City, and downtown Cleveland regularly sell for $300K to $500K+. Cleveland offers extraordinary value for cash buyers and rental investors, but appreciation is slower than Columbus or Cincinnati.
What's Ohio's Owner-Occupancy Credit and how do I get it?
The Owner-Occupancy Credit (OOC) provides a 2.5% reduction in voted property taxes on primary residences in Ohio. Modest savings typically $60–$100 per year on a median Ohio home. Free to file with your county auditor — most counties allow online filing. NOT automatic. Combines with the Homestead Exemption ($25K taxable value reduction for 65+/disabled) for stacked savings. Set a calendar reminder for week 4 after closing to file both.
Is Ohio a good place to buy investment property?
Ohio has been a popular state for out-of-state real estate investors due to low entry prices and strong rent-to-price ratios. Cleveland and Toledo offer some of the best cash flow numbers in America. Risks include slow appreciation in struggling neighborhoods, higher tenant turnover, significant maintenance costs on older housing stock, and Ohio's RITA/CCA tax compliance complexity for non-resident owners. Stronger markets like Columbus suburbs (Dublin, Powell, Westerville) and Cincinnati suburbs (Mason, West Chester) offer a better balance of cash flow and appreciation but at higher entry cost.
What's the best Ohio city for first-time buyers?
Depends on your budget and industry. For affordable starter homes: Akron ($155K), Dayton ($155K), and Toledo ($135K) offer real entry points under $200K. For strong appreciation potential and tech/healthcare jobs: Columbus ($285K) is Ohio's hottest growth market. For balanced affordability with growing job market: Cincinnati ($235K) with strong corporate base (P&G, Kroger, Western & Southern). Run all three through our budget planner with your specific income and don't forget to add the city income tax in your work location.

Understanding mortgages in Washington

Washington is a state of extreme regional contrasts when it comes to housing. The statewide median home price of approximately $625,000 hides dramatic variation — the Seattle metro area (King, Snohomish, and northern Pierce counties) commands $750,000–$1.5M+ medians thanks to Microsoft, Amazon, Boeing, T-Mobile, Costco, and Starbucks headquarters. Meanwhile, Spokane ($375K), Tacoma ($450K), Vancouver WA ($475K), Bellingham ($525K), and Yakima ($325K) remain affordable compared to most West Coast cities.

The current 30-year fixed mortgage rate in Washington averages approximately 6.55%, near the national average. Washington has one of the strongest credit union ecosystems in America, led by BECU (Boeing Employees Credit Union, the 4th largest credit union in the country with 1.5M+ members), Washington State Employees Credit Union, Sound Credit Union, and Numerica they often offer rates 0.15–0.25% below national lenders, particularly for Boeing, Microsoft, and government employees.

Why Seattle and the Eastside are so much more expensive

The Seattle metro and Eastside have housing markets that look more like the Bay Area than the rest of Washington. Median home prices in Seattle now exceed $870,000, while Bellevue sits at $1,250,000, Redmond at $1,100,000, and Mercer Island over $2,000,000. The region has unusually high household incomes ($150K–$250K median in many tech ZIP codes) driven by Microsoft (Redmond), Amazon (Seattle), Boeing (Renton, Everett), T-Mobile (Bellevue), Costco (Issaquah), Starbucks (Seattle SODO), and hundreds of tech startups in South Lake Union and Bellevue Spring District.

Washington's biggest financial advantage for homebuyers is zero state income tax. A software engineer earning $200,000 in Seattle saves roughly $14,000–$16,000 per year in income tax compared to California's 9.3% bracket. Over a 30year mortgage, that's $420,000+ in income tax savings enough to fully fund a down payment plus accelerate mortgage payoff. The tradeoff: Washington has higher sales tax (10.3% in Seattle), higher gas tax (49.4¢/gallon among the highest), and a Business & Occupation (B&O) tax that affects small business owners. For W-2 high earners, the math strongly favors Washington over California, Oregon, or other high-tax West Coast states.

Washington property tax by county

Washington's effective property tax rate of 0.92% is near the national average. For a median $625,000 Washington home, that's approximately $5,750 per year, or $479 per month. Critically, Washington's Initiative 747 caps property tax annual increases at 1% per year (well below typical inflation of 2–3%), making property tax relatively predictable. Variation between counties matters — Spokane County's effective rate (1.09%) is 18% higher than King County's (0.92%).

Washington's senior and disabled property tax exemptions

Washington offers a Senior Citizen and Disabled Persons Property Tax Exemption for homeowners age 61+ or permanently disabled with annual household income under approximately $58,000 (the exact threshold varies by county and is adjusted annually). The benefit ranges from a property tax reduction to a complete freeze on assessed value typically saving $2,000–$5,000 per year depending on home value and income tier. Washington also offers a Disabled Veteran Property Tax Exemption for veterans with a VA disability rating of 80% or higher (or surviving spouses), with benefit levels tied to disability percentage and income. Apply through your county assessor's office with VA disability documentation and proof of income.

One Washington advantage: Initiative 747 (passed in 2001) limits regular property tax levies to a 1% annual increase, far below typical inflation. This makes long-term property tax costs more predictable than in states without such caps. The tradeoff is that voter-approved levies (school bonds, parks, transportation) can push your actual bill higher — review your county assessor's detailed breakdown before buying.

Washington first-time homebuyer programs (WSHFC)

The Washington State Housing Finance Commission (WSHFC) offers some of the strongest first time buyer programs on the West Coast. Use our 401(k) calculator to balance saving for the down payment against retirement contributions.

Home Advantage Program

Below-market 30-year fixed mortgage rates for first time buyers (or non-first-time in certain target areas). Combines with optional Home Advantage Down Payment Assistance — a second mortgage of up to 4% of the loan amount at low fixed rate. Maximum income limits ~$160K in King County, lower elsewhere. Minimum 620 FICO score. Statewide most popular WSHFC program.

House Key Opportunity

The original WSHFC program for true first-time buyers (no homeownership in past 3 years) at or below 80% Area Median Income. Below-market mortgage rates plus optional second mortgage for down payment. Requires homebuyer education class. Income limits significantly lower than Home Advantage designed for moderate-income workers.

House Key Veterans

Enhanced WSHFC program for veterans, active duty military, and surviving spouses. Provides additional down payment assistance on top of standard Home Advantage benefits, plus reduced fees. Can be combined with VA loans for maximum benefit zero down payment plus WSHFC assistance for closing costs. Available statewide through WSHFC participating lenders.

Opportunity Downpayment Assistance Loan

Up to $15,000 second mortgage at 1% interest to cover down payment and closing costs. Available for buyers using WSHFC first mortgage with income at or below 80% AMI. Particularly valuable for Seattle metro buyers facing high down payment requirements. Apply through participating WSHFC lenders such as BECU, Banner Bank, or U.S. Bank.

Frequently Asked Questions
What is the average mortgage rate in Washington in 2026?
As of June 2026, Washington 30year fixed mortgage rates average approximately 6.55% near the national average of 6.53%. The Seattle metro sees competitive rates due to high-income tech workers and strong credit profiles. Washington credit unions (BECU, WSECU, Sound Credit Union, Numerica) often offer rates 0.15 to 0.25% below national lenders, especially for Boeing, Microsoft, and government employees. Borrowers with credit scores above 740 typically see additional rate reductions of 0.25–0.5%.
Why is Seattle and the Eastside so much more expensive than the rest of Washington?
The Seattle metro and Eastside are among the most expensive housing markets in America, with median home prices ranging $750K–$1.5M+. The region hosts Microsoft (Redmond), Amazon (Seattle), Boeing (Renton/Everett), T-Mobile (Bellevue), Costco (Issaquah), and Starbucks (Seattle), plus hundreds of tech startups. Rest of Washington is dramatically different — Spokane medians around $375K, Tacoma $450K, Vancouver $475K, Yakima under $325K. If you can work remotely or your employer allows hybrid, the cost difference is substantial.
What is the no state income tax advantage in Washington?
Washington is one of only 9 US states with NO state income tax. For a tech worker earning $150K in Washington, this saves approximately $7,500–$9,000/year vs California or Oregon. Over a 30-year mortgage period, that's $225K–$270K in income tax savings — money that can go toward higher home prices, faster mortgage payoff, or retirement. The tradeoff: Washington has higher sales tax (~10.3% in Seattle) and property tax than no-income-tax peers like Texas, but the math still strongly favors high earners. VA loans are still available — ZERO down + no PMI for eligible veterans, active duty, National Guard, and Reservists.
What are Washington's senior and disabled veteran property tax exemptions?
Washington offers a Senior Citizen and Disabled Persons Property Tax Exemption for homeowners age 61+ or permanently disabled with annual household income under approximately $58,000 (varies by county). The exemption reduces or freezes property taxes — saving $2,000–$5,000 per year depending on home value and income tier. Washington also offers a Disabled Veteran Property Tax Exemption for veterans with VA disability ratings of 80%+, with benefit levels tied to disability percentage and income. Apply through your county assessor.
Does Washington have any state or city income tax?
No — Washington has ZERO state income tax and zero city income tax. Seattle attempted to impose a high-earner income tax in 2017 but it was struck down. Washington is one of only 9 US states with no income tax joining Texas, Florida, Tennessee, Nevada, and a few others. The state funds itself through sales tax (~10.3% in Seattle, among the highest in America), high gas tax (49.4¢/gallon), and a Business & Occupation (B&O) tax on businesses. For homeowners, this means more take-home pay goes toward your mortgage payment.
What's the best Washington city for first-time buyers?
Depends on your industry and budget. For maximum affordability: Yakima ($325K) and Spokane ($375K) offer real entry points under $400K. For balanced affordability + strong job market: Tacoma ($450K) and Vancouver WA ($475K) have growing economies plus easy access to Seattle and Portland respectively. For high salaries despite high prices: Seattle/Eastside — but pair with WSHFC programs to manage the down payment. Bellingham ($525K) offers natural beauty and a college town vibe at moderate prices. Run all options through our budget planner with your specific income.

What Is Net Worth?

Net worth is the simplest formula in personal finance. Take everything you own (your assets) and subtract everything you owe (your liabilities). What is left is your net worth.

If you have $400,000 in assets and $250,000 in liabilities, your net worth is $150,000. If you have $100,000 in assets and $130,000 in liabilities, your net worth is negative $30,000.

That is it. No tricks. No magic.

But what surprises most people is how few Americans actually know their net worth. A 2024 survey by Schwab found that only 32 percent of Americans calculate their net worth annually. That means two thirds of households are flying blind on the most important financial metric they have.

Tracking your net worth monthly or quarterly gives you a clear picture of whether you are moving forward. A high salary means nothing if your net worth stays flat. A modest salary that grows your net worth by $20,000 per year is the real win.

How to Calculate Your Net Worth (Step by Step)

The calculator above does the math for you. If you want to understand what is happening behind the scenes, here is the five step process.

Step 1: List All Your Assets

Assets are anything you own that has cash value. The key categories:

Liquid Assets (easy to access): Cash on hand, checking account balances, savings account balances, money market accounts, high yield savings account balances.

Investments (longer term but accessible): Brokerage account balances, individual stocks, mutual funds, ETFs, bonds and treasury bills.

Retirement Accounts (locked until age 59 and a half): 401(k) balances, Traditional IRA balances, Roth IRA balances, 403(b), 457(b), TSP balances, pension lump sum value.

Real Estate: Primary home market value, rental property market value, vacation home value.

Vehicles (use Kelley Blue Book private sale value): Cars, trucks, motorcycles, boats, RVs.

Other Assets: Business equity, cash value of life insurance, valuable collectibles (with documented appraisal), cryptocurrency holdings, precious metals.

Step 2: List All Your Liabilities

Liabilities are anything you owe. Total balance, not monthly payment.

Secured Debt (backed by an asset): Mortgage balance, home equity loan balance, auto loan balances.

Unsecured Debt: Credit card balances (not credit limits), personal loan balances, medical debt, student loan balances.

Other Liabilities: Tax debt owed to IRS or state, money owed to family or friends, business debt you personally guaranteed.

Step 3: Add Up Your Total Assets

Sum every asset category. This is your gross worth.

Step 4: Add Up Your Total Liabilities

Sum every debt. This is your total debt.

Step 5: Subtract Liabilities From Assets

Assets minus liabilities equals net worth.

Example: Total assets: $475,000 (home $350K, retirement $80K, savings $25K, car $20K). Total liabilities: $295,000 (mortgage $270K, car loan $15K, credit cards $10K). Net worth: $180,000.

Why Average and Median Are So Different

Notice the gap between median and average. The average is much higher because of a few extremely wealthy households pulling the number up. The median is what the typical household actually has.

Use the median number for honest comparison. If you are 40 years old with $135,000 net worth, you are doing better than half of Americans your age. That is the real benchmark.

What "On Track" Looks Like

Personal finance benchmarks for net worth by age have a popular rule of thumb. Multiply your age by your pre-tax income, then divide by 10. That is roughly where your net worth should be.

Example: Age 35, earning $70,000 per year. Target net worth: 35 x 70,000 / 10 = $245,000.

This formula breaks down at younger ages (a 25 year old at $50,000 income would target $125,000, which is unrealistic for most). It works better starting around age 35.

The Fidelity savings rule offers another benchmark for retirement: 1x your annual salary saved by age 30, 3x by age 40, 6x by age 50, 8x by age 60, and 10x by age 67. Note these are retirement savings benchmarks specifically, not full net worth, but they help calibrate progress.

Total vs Liquid vs Investable Net Worth

Most calculators just show total net worth. Smart investors track three different numbers.

Total Net Worth

Everything you own minus everything you owe. Includes home equity, vehicles, collectibles. This is the full picture.

Liquid Net Worth

Total net worth minus your home equity and other illiquid assets. This represents money you could actually access in an emergency without selling your house.

Why this matters: A retiree with $800,000 net worth but $600,000 of it tied up in their primary home has only $200,000 of liquid net worth. That changes everything about how they should plan retirement income.

Investable Net Worth

Cash and investment accounts only. Excludes home, vehicles, retirement accounts, and personal property. This is the money you can deploy into investments today.

The calculator above shows all three numbers automatically.

Why Some People Have Negative Net Worth (And Why That Is Normal)

If your calculation comes out negative, do not panic. Negative net worth is more common than people realize.

Who Typically Has Negative Net Worth

Recent graduates with $80,000+ in student loans and few assets. A new doctor or lawyer can easily start their career $200,000 in the negative.

Young homeowners who just bought a home with a small down payment. If you put 5 percent down on a $400,000 house, your mortgage is $380,000 but your equity is just $20,000. Combined with closing costs and any other debt, you might be net worth negative for a year or two.

Anyone in early career who has not had time to accumulate assets but does have student loans, car loans, or credit card debt.

When Negative Net Worth Is a Problem

Negative net worth becomes a real problem when you are over 35 and still negative, the negative number is growing each year (not shrinking), or the cause is consumer debt (credit cards, personal loans) not investment debt (student loans for a degree paying off, mortgage on appreciating asset).

The goal is not to be positive immediately. The goal is for your net worth to be growing every year, even if it is growing from negative $40,000 to negative $20,000 to zero.

How to Increase Your Net Worth: 5 Strategies

Knowing your net worth means nothing if you do not have a plan to grow it. Here are the five highest impact strategies, ranked by typical effectiveness.

  1. Maximize Employer 401(k) Match (Free Money)

    If your employer matches 401(k) contributions and you are not contributing enough to get the full match, you are leaving free money on the table. A 100 percent match on the first 6 percent of salary is roughly 6 percent of your income going straight into your net worth every year. On a $60,000 salary, that is $3,600 per year of pure net worth growth. Use our 401(k) calculator to project this.

  2. Pay Down High Interest Debt First

    Every dollar paid off on a 22 percent APR credit card is a guaranteed 22 percent return. The stock market averages 10 percent historically. There is no investment that beats paying off credit card debt. Order: credit cards first, then personal loans, then car loans, then student loans, then mortgage. Use our debt payoff calculator to plan your strategy.

  3. Automate Investment Contributions

    The single biggest predictor of long term net worth growth is automation. Set up automatic transfers from checking to investment accounts the day after each paycheck. Even $200 per month invested at average market returns of 8 percent grows to $293,000 over 30 years. Use our investment calculator to project compound growth.

  4. Increase Income, Not Just Cut Spending

    You can only cut spending so far. Income has no ceiling. A $5,000 raise invested at market returns is worth roughly $370,000 to your net worth by retirement age, when compounded over 35 years. Practical strategies: negotiate annually, switch jobs every 2 to 4 years, add a side income stream, develop high value skills.

  5. Build an Emergency Fund First

    Net worth growth is fragile if you have no emergency fund. One job loss or medical emergency without savings can wipe out years of progress when you charge expenses to high interest credit cards. Read our complete emergency fund guide for how much to save and where to keep it.

Common Net Worth Tracking Mistakes

Even people who calculate their net worth often make these mistakes.

  1. Overvaluing Your Home

    Your home is worth what someone will pay for it today, not what Zillow estimates or what you paid for it. Be conservative. Use the lowest of recent comparable sales, tax assessed value, or Zillow estimate minus 10 percent. Selling a house costs roughly 6 to 8 percent in agent fees plus closing costs.

  2. Counting Credit Limits as Assets

    Your credit cards have a $50,000 combined limit. That is not net worth. That is your borrowing capacity. Only what you have actually saved or invested counts as an asset.

  3. Forgetting About Retirement Accounts

    Many people exclude their 401(k) from net worth because they "cannot touch it." This is wrong. Your 401(k) is your money. Include the full balance in your net worth calculation.

  4. Counting Gross Salary Instead of Take Home

    When evaluating how net worth growth fits your income, use take home pay (after taxes, after 401k contributions, after health insurance). Use our paycheck calculator to see your real take home pay.

  5. Tracking Too Often

    Checking your net worth daily creates anxiety. Markets move. Home values fluctuate. The number bounces around. Monthly tracking is enough for most people. Quarterly is fine if monthly stresses you out. Only the trend matters, not the daily noise.

  6. Counting Future Money

    Your $80,000 expected inheritance is not net worth. Your $50,000 promised year end bonus is not net worth. Only count money that is already yours, sitting in accounts you control.

  7. Not Updating Liabilities

    People update assets (their 401(k) grew) but forget to update liabilities. Pull your actual mortgage balance, credit card balance, and loan balances from current statements. Old numbers create false net worth.

How Often Should You Check Your Net Worth?

Most personal finance experts recommend monthly tracking. Here is why and how.

Monthly Tracking (Recommended)

Pick the same date each month (1st of the month works well). Update all account balances and recalculate. Track in a simple spreadsheet or use our calculator.

Benefits: See trends emerging early, notice when one category is shrinking (or growing), catch banking errors faster, build awareness of money habits. Time required: 15 to 30 minutes per month.

Quarterly Tracking (Acceptable Minimum)

If monthly feels excessive, quarterly is fine. Pick a fixed day (end of each quarter works). Same process. Time required: 20 to 40 minutes per quarter.

Annual Tracking (Bare Minimum)

The minimum acceptable cadence. Do it on a memorable date (January 1st, your birthday, tax day). You will lose some insight but at least you stay aware.

What Is Net Worth?

Net worth is the plainest number in personal finance: add up everything you own, subtract everything you owe, and the difference is your net worth. Nothing else to it.

Own $400,000 in assets against $250,000 in debts and your net worth is $150,000. Own $100,000 against $130,000 in debts and it is negative $30,000. The formula never changes; only the inputs do.

What is striking is how few people actually track it. Surveys repeatedly find that only about a third of Americans work out their net worth in a given year, which means most households are steering by their paycheck alone, the least reliable gauge of real progress.

A big salary can hide a stalled net worth, and a modest one can quietly build a large one. Checking the number every month or quarter is what tells you which of those is happening to you.

How Do You Calculate Personal Net Worth?

The calculator handles the arithmetic, but the net worth formula calculation is worth understanding. It is five short steps.

Step 1: List everything you own

Assets are anything with real cash value. Group them so nothing slips through: liquid holdings (checking, savings, money-market, high-yield savings); investments (brokerage balances, individual stocks, funds, ETFs, bonds); retirement (401k, Traditional and Roth IRA, 403b, 457, TSP, pension cash value); real estate at current market value; vehicles at private-sale value; and other holdings like business equity, life-insurance cash value, or documented collectibles.

Step 2: List everything you owe

Liabilities are your outstanding balances, not your monthly payments. Split them into secured debt tied to an asset (mortgage, home-equity loan, auto loans) and unsecured debt (credit cards at the balance, not the limit; personal loans; medical debt; student loans), plus anything else you genuinely owe, such as tax debt or money borrowed from family.

Step 3 to 5: Add, add, subtract

Total the assets, total the liabilities, and subtract the second from the first. That single number is your net worth.

An example net worth statement: assets of $475,000 (home $350K, retirement $80K, savings $25K, car $20K) minus liabilities of $295,000 (mortgage $270K, car loan $15K, cards $10K) equals a net worth of $180,000. Writing it out this way, as a one-page net worth statement, is exactly what the free spreadsheet above does for you.

How to Read Your Results

The headline card

The big number is your total net worth. It turns emerald when positive and red when negative, so the sign registers before the digits do. Early in a career, red is normal, not a failure.

Underneath, you get liquid net worth (what you could actually reach in a hurry), investable net worth (your total investable assets, meaning cash plus investment accounts), and your debt-to-asset ratio. Why the average and median look so far apart

At every age the average towers over the median, and that gap is the most misunderstood thing in net worth statistics. The average gets dragged upward by a small number of very rich households, while the median, the household sitting exactly in the middle, describes where most people really are. If you are 40 with $135,000, you are ahead of half of Americans your age, whatever the scarier average suggests. For context, the overall median rose about 37 percent (after inflation) between 2019 and 2022, the largest jump the survey has recorded, driven mostly by rising home values.

What "on track" looks like

One popular yardstick, sometimes called the millionaire next door calculator rule, multiplies your age by your pre-tax income and divides by ten. At 35 earning $70,000, that points to roughly $245,000. It is unrealistic for people in their twenties, so treat it as a rough guide that only starts to make sense around 35. Fidelity's savings-multiple rule is a separate retirement benchmark: about 1x salary saved by 30, 3x by 40, 6x by 50, 8x by 60, and 10x by 67.

Total, Liquid, and Investable Net Worth

Most tools stop at one number. Comparing net worth and liquid net worth, plus a third figure, tells you far more about your actual flexibility.

Total net worth

Everything you own minus everything you owe, home equity and vehicles included. The complete picture, and the number the age benchmarks use.

Liquid net worth

Total net worth stripped of your home and other assets you cannot sell quickly. This is what you could actually put your hands on in a crisis. A retiree worth $800,000 with $600,000 locked in their house has only $200,000 in liquid net worth, and that single distinction reshapes how they should plan retirement income.

Investable net worth

Cash and investment accounts only, excluding your home, car, and retirement balances. These are your total investable assets, the money you could deploy today. The calculator reports all three figures side by side.

Tangible and Adjusted Net Worth

Two related figures come up in lending and business, and both are easy to work out once you know your standard net worth.

Tangible net worth calculation

Tangible net worth removes assets you cannot physically hold or easily sell, things like goodwill, patents, and other intangibles. For a household this rarely changes much, but if you own a business it matters. To do the tangible net worth calculation, take your total net worth and subtract the value of any intangible assets. Lenders often use a related figure, debt to tangible net worth, to judge how much real, sellable backing sits behind your obligations. So how to calculate tangible net worth is simply: total assets, minus intangible assets, minus total liabilities.

Adjusted net worth

Adjusted net worth restates your assets at more conservative or current values rather than book values, for example marking a home to a realistic sale price after selling costs, or discounting a hard-to-sell collectible. It is the honest version of your balance sheet, and it is usually a little lower than the headline number. Using adjusted values is why the mistakes section below warns against overvaluing your home.

Negative Net Worth: Common and Fixable

If your total comes out below zero, do not panic. Negative net worth is far more common than people admit, and for many it is simply a stage.

Who tends to be negative

Recent graduates carrying large student loans with few assets yet; a newly qualified doctor can start out $200,000 in the hole. New homeowners who bought with a small down payment, where a 5 percent deposit on a $400,000 home leaves a $380,000 mortgage against $20,000 of equity. And anyone early in their career who has debts but has not had time to build assets.

When it is actually a problem

Negative net worth turns worrying when you are past 35 and still below zero, when the number grows each year instead of shrinking, or when the cause is consumer debt like credit cards rather than debt attached to something that builds value, such as a mortgage or a degree. The target is never to be positive overnight; it is for the line to climb every year, even from negative $40,000 to negative $20,000 to zero.

How to Increase Your Net Worth: 5 Strategies

Knowing the number is pointless without a plan to grow it. Here are the five moves with the most leverage, roughly in order.

  1. Capture the full employer 401(k) match

    An unclaimed match is the only guaranteed return you will ever be offered. A 100 percent match on the first 6 percent of pay adds roughly 6 percent of your salary to your net worth every year, about $3,600 on a $60,000 income, before your own money even compounds. Our 401(k) calculator projects it.

  2. Clear high-interest debt first

    Wiping out a 22 percent credit card is a guaranteed 22 percent return, and no investment reliably matches that. Work in order: credit cards, then personal loans, then car loans, then student loans, then the mortgage. Our debt payoff calculator maps the timeline.

  3. Automate your investing

    The strongest predictor of long-run net worth is not income, it is automation. Schedule transfers into investments for the day after payday so saving is never a decision. Even $200 a month at an 8 percent average return grows to roughly $293,000 over 30 years. Our investment calculator shows the curve.

  4. Grow income, not just cut costs

    Spending cuts have a floor; income does not. A single $5,000 raise, invested, is worth around $370,000 by retirement once compounded over 35 years. Negotiate yearly, change jobs every few years, and build skills that command more.

  5. Build the emergency fund first

    Net worth growth is fragile without a cushion. One job loss or medical bill charged to a credit card can undo years of progress. See our emergency fund calculator for how much to hold and where to keep it.

Common Net Worth Tracking Mistakes

Even diligent trackers slip on these.

  1. Overvaluing your home

    Your home is worth what a buyer will pay today, not what an estimate flatters you with or what you paid. Use the lowest of recent comparable sales, the assessed value, or an online estimate minus about 10 percent, and remember selling costs run 6 to 8 percent.

  2. Counting credit limits as assets

    A $50,000 combined card limit is borrowing capacity, not wealth. Only money you have actually saved or invested counts.

  3. Leaving out retirement accounts

    Skipping your 401(k) because you "can't touch it" understates your net worth badly. It is your money; include the full balance.

  4. Using gross instead of take-home pay

    When you judge how well your net worth tracks your income, use take-home pay, not gross. Our paycheck calculator shows your real number.

  5. Checking it too often

    Daily checks just feed anxiety as markets and home values wobble. Monthly is plenty; quarterly is fine. Only the trend matters, not the noise.

  6. Counting money that is not yours yet

    An expected inheritance or a promised bonus is not net worth. Count only what already sits in accounts you control.

  7. Forgetting to update debts

    People refresh their asset balances but leave old loan figures in place. Pull current statements for every debt, or your net worth will look better than it is.

How Often Should You Track Net Worth?

For most people, tracking net worth once a month hits the sweet spot. Here is how each cadence works.

Monthly (recommended)

Pick a fixed date, the 1st works well, refresh every balance, and recalculate. It takes 15 to 30 minutes and it surfaces trends early, flags a shrinking category before it becomes a habit, and catches the occasional bank error. The free spreadsheet above is built for exactly this rhythm.

Quarterly (acceptable)

If monthly feels like too much, tracking net worth every quarter still works. Same process, four times a year, 20 to 40 minutes each.

Annually (bare minimum)

Once a year on a memorable date keeps you at least aware. You lose the early signals, but you stay honest with yourself.

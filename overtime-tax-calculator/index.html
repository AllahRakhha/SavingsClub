What "No Tax on Overtime" Actually Means in 2026

The phrase makes it sound like overtime pay became tax-free. It did not. What the law created is a federal income tax deduction for qualified overtime, added by the 2025 tax act and in effect for the 2025 through 2028 tax years. It is retroactive to January 1, 2025, and it is scheduled to expire after 2028 unless Congress extends it.

A deduction lowers the income you are taxed on. It does not hand you your overtime back tax-free. If you deduct $5,000 of overtime premium and you are in the 22 percent bracket, you save about $1,100 in federal income tax, not $5,000. That is the single biggest misunderstanding about this rule, and the calculator above is built to show you the real number.

Only the Half-Premium Qualifies (Not Your Whole Overtime Check)

This is where most people, and most calculators, get it wrong. The deduction applies only to the premium portion required by the Fair Labor Standards Act, which is the extra half of time-and-a-half pay. It is not your full overtime paycheck.

The formula: Deductible premium = your regular rate × 0.5 × overtime hours. If your regular rate is $20 and overtime pays $30 an hour, only the extra $10 per hour is deductible, not the full $30.

If you are paid double time (2x), it does not help you here. Only the FLSA-required half-premium qualifies. The extra amount above time-and-a-half is not deductible under current IRS guidance, so your total pay goes up but your deductible amount stays the same. The calculator handles this automatically: pick double time and it still deducts only the qualifying half.

The Cap and the Income Phase-Out

Two limits decide how much of your premium you can actually deduct.

The annual cap

You can deduct at most $12,500 if you file single or head of household, and up to $25,000 if you are married filing jointly. For a married couple, the $25,000 is the combined limit for both spouses, not $25,000 each.

The MAGI phase-out

The deduction shrinks once your modified adjusted gross income (MAGI) passes $150,000 for single and head of household filers, or $300,000 for joint filers. For every $1,000 of MAGI above the threshold, your maximum deduction drops by $100. It reaches zero at about $275,000 (single) or $550,000 (joint). For most workers, MAGI is simply the income on your Form 1040.

Worked example: Elena is single with $10,000 of qualified overtime premium and a MAGI of $170,000. That is $20,000 over the threshold, so her deduction is reduced by $20,000 ÷ $1,000 × $100 = $2,000. She can deduct $8,000 instead of the full $10,000.

What the Deduction Does Not Remove

Even when you qualify for the full deduction, your overtime is not free of every tax.

  • Social Security and Medicare (FICA) still apply. Your employer still withholds 6.2 percent for Social Security (up to the annual wage base) and 1.45 percent for Medicare on your full overtime pay, including the premium. The deduction only touches federal income tax.
  • Many states still tax overtime in full. This is a federal deduction. Whether your state follows it depends on state conformity rules. As of early 2026, several states with their own income tax codes, including California and New York, had not adopted it, so your state may still tax the whole amount.
  • Your paycheck may not change during the year. Employers generally keep withholding federal income tax on overtime as usual. The benefit typically shows up when you file, as a smaller tax bill or a larger refund, not as a bigger weekly check.

Who Qualifies, and Who Does Not

The rule is narrower than the headlines suggest.

You generally qualify if you are an hourly or other non-exempt employee who is covered by the FLSA and receives time-and-a-half for hours over 40 in a workweek.

You generally do not qualify if any of these apply: you are married and file separately; you are a salaried employee who is exempt from FLSA overtime; your extra pay comes only from a state law or union agreement rather than the FLSA; or your MAGI is above the phase-out ceiling. Most true independent contractors do not qualify either, because the overtime must be FLSA-required.

How to Find and Claim Your Qualified Overtime

You claim the deduction on Schedule 1-A, a form that attaches to Form 1040, and you can take it whether you itemize or use the standard deduction.

For the 2025 tax year (filed in 2026)

Employers were not required to break out overtime separately, so your qualified amount may appear in Box 14 of your W-2, on a separate employer statement, or not at all. If it is not provided, you calculate it from your pay stubs: total your FLSA overtime hours and multiply by your regular rate times 0.5.

For the 2026 tax year and later

Employers must report your qualified overtime compensation on your W-2 in Box 12 using code TT, so you can transfer that figure straight to Schedule 1-A. Always check the number against your own records to be sure it captures your full benefit.

Real Examples

Warehouse worker, single

Earns $22 an hour, works about 8 overtime hours a week for 50 weeks. The deductible premium is $22 × 0.5 × 400 hours = $4,400. With a MAGI well under $150,000 and a 12 percent bracket, that is roughly $528 in federal tax saved. The full overtime pay stays on the W-2 and still owes FICA.

Nurse paid double time, single

Earns $40 an hour and picks up double-time shifts. Even though she is paid 2x, only the FLSA half-premium counts, so her deductible amount is $40 × 0.5 × her overtime hours, not the full double-time pay. Choosing "double time" in the calculator shows this gap clearly.

Couple near the phase-out, married filing jointly

Combined MAGI of $340,000 with $25,000 of overtime premium between them. They are $40,000 over the $300,000 threshold, so the cap drops by $40,000 ÷ $1,000 × $100 = $4,000. Their deduction is $21,000 rather than the full $25,000.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is overtime really not taxed in 2026?

Not exactly. The No Tax on Overtime provision is a federal income tax deduction, not a full exemption. For tax years 2025 through 2028, eligible workers can deduct the FLSA-required overtime premium (the extra half of time-and-a-half pay) from their federal taxable income, up to a yearly cap. Your overtime is still subject to Social Security and Medicare taxes, and often state income tax.

How much overtime can I deduct?

You can deduct your qualified overtime premium up to $12,500 if you are single or head of household, or up to $25,000 if you are married filing jointly. Only the half-time premium counts, not your full overtime pay. The deduction also phases out once your modified adjusted gross income passes $150,000 (single) or $300,000 (joint).

What counts as qualified overtime for the deduction?

Only the premium portion required by the Fair Labor Standards Act qualifies, which is the extra 0.5 times your regular rate on hours over 40 in a week. If your regular rate is $20 and overtime pays $30, only the $10 premium per hour is deductible, not the full $30. If you are paid double time, only the FLSA half-premium qualifies, not the extra amount above time-and-a-half.

Does the No Tax on Overtime deduction remove Social Security and Medicare taxes?

No. The deduction only reduces federal income tax. Social Security tax of 6.2 percent (up to the annual wage base) and Medicare tax of 1.45 percent still apply to your full overtime pay, including the premium portion. Many states also continue to tax overtime in full, because not every state has adopted the federal change.

Who does not qualify for the overtime deduction?

Married people who file separately are not eligible. Salaried employees who are exempt from FLSA overtime generally do not qualify, and neither does extra pay that comes only from state law or a union agreement rather than the FLSA. Workers whose modified adjusted gross income is above $275,000 (single) or $550,000 (joint) are fully phased out.

How do I claim the No Tax on Overtime deduction?

You claim it on Schedule 1-A, which attaches to Form 1040, and you can take it whether you itemize or use the standard deduction. For the 2025 tax year, your qualified overtime may appear in Box 14 of your W-2 or on a separate employer statement, or you may need to calculate it from pay stubs. For 2026 and later, employers report it on your W-2 in Box 12 using code TT.

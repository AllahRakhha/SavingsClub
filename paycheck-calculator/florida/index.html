    How Florida paycheck taxes work in 2026

    The Florida paycheck calculator above skips a step that most states force on you, and that step is the whole story. Florida has no state income tax. Its constitution actually bans one, so this is not a temporary break that a future legislature can quietly remove. That single fact is why a Florida worker keeps more of the same salary than a worker in almost any other state.

    Our SavingsClub Research Team built this Florida paycheck calculator to help everyday workers see their true take-home pay without wading through confusing payroll jargon. When you run the numbers, only three deductions show up: federal income tax, Social Security, and Medicare. There is no line for state tax, because there is nothing to put there.

    What actually gets taken out of a Florida paycheck

    Because Florida sits out the state income tax game, your deductions come down to federal rules that apply everywhere:

    • Federal income tax. This is based on the 2026 federal brackets and your standard deduction, which is 16,100 dollars for a single filer and 32,200 dollars for a married couple filing jointly. Only the income above your deduction gets taxed, and only the portion inside each bracket is taxed at that bracket rate.
    • Social Security. You pay 6.2 percent of your wages, up to a wage base of 184,500 dollars in 2026. Earnings above that base are not charged Social Security, which is why very high earners see this line stop growing.
    • Medicare. You pay 1.45 percent of all wages with no cap. High earners pay an extra 0.9 percent on income above 200,000 dollars for single filers, or 250,000 dollars for joint filers.

    That is the full list. No county income tax, no city income tax, no state disability deduction. This matters because the fewer mandatory subtractions your paycheck has, the more of your gross pay lands in your bank account. If you want to see the federal side broken out on its own, our national paycheck calculator shows the same math without any state assumptions.

    A worked example: 75,000 dollars in Florida

    Numbers make this concrete. Take a single filer earning 75,000 dollars in 2026 with no 401(k) contributions. Here is exactly how the paycheck breaks down.

    LineAmount

    Social Security (6.2%)$4,650
    Medicare (1.45%)$1,088

    That works out to roughly 2,369 dollars every two weeks. The reason this take-home is high is not a trick or a loophole. It is simply that the state line reads zero. The same paycheck in a state with an income tax would have one more subtraction, and that subtraction is real money.

    Florida versus states that do tax income

    The clearest way to understand Florida take-home pay is to compare it against states with a flat income tax, since the difference is easy to see. On a 75,000 dollar salary in 2026, here is what the same single filer keeps in each state.

    A Florida resident keeps about 2,484 dollars more per year than a North Carolina resident and about 3,568 dollars more than an Illinois resident, on the exact same salary. Over a decade, that Illinois gap alone adds up to more than 35,000 dollars. You can check each of these yourself with our North Carolina paycheck calculator, Michigan paycheck calculator, and Illinois paycheck calculator.

    An honest caveat. A bigger paycheck does not automatically mean a cheaper life. Florida makes up some of its budget through property taxes, sales tax, and notably high home insurance costs. So while your take-home pay is higher, your total cost of living depends on where in Florida you settle and how you spend. Higher income kept is a real advantage, but it is one piece of the picture, not the whole thing.

    How to lower your Florida tax and keep even more

    Since there is no state tax lever to pull in Florida, the way to reduce what you owe is on the federal side. The most direct tool is pre-tax retirement savings.

    When you contribute to a traditional 401(k), that money comes out before federal income tax is calculated, so it lowers your taxable income right now. The 2026 employee contribution limit is 24,500 dollars. On a 75,000 dollar salary, contributing 10 percent means 7,500 dollars moves out of taxable income and into your own retirement account, which trims your federal tax and builds your future at the same time. Use the what-if slider above to watch this happen, then dig deeper with our 401(k) calculator and retirement calculator.

    A few other federal moves that help Florida workers keep more:

    • Contribute to an HSA if you have a high-deductible health plan. Those dollars are pre-tax and grow tax-free for medical costs.
    • Adjust your W-4 withholding so you are not lending the government an interest-free loan through a giant refund. A refund feels good, but it means too much was withheld all year.
    • Track your effective tax rate, not just your bracket. Your bracket is the rate on your last dollar. Your effective rate, shown in your results above, is what you actually pay across all your income.

    Florida paycheck questions people ask

    Does Florida have a state income tax?
    No. Florida is one of only nine states with no state income tax, and its constitution bans one. There is also no county or city income tax anywhere in Florida. That means the only taxes withheld from a Florida paycheck are federal income tax, Social Security, and Medicare.
    How much is taken out of a Florida paycheck?
    For most workers, the deductions are federal income tax, Social Security at 6.2 percent up to the 184,500 dollar wage base, and Medicare at 1.45 percent. On a 75,000 dollar salary in 2026, a single filer keeps about 61,592 dollars per year after those deductions, which works out to roughly 2,369 dollars every two weeks.
    How do I calculate my Florida take-home pay?
    Start with your gross pay, subtract any pre-tax retirement or benefit contributions, then subtract federal income tax, Social Security, and Medicare. Because Florida has no state income tax, there is nothing more to subtract at the state level. The calculator on this page does all of that math for you using the 2026 federal brackets.
    Is Florida a good state for take-home pay?
    For the same salary, a Florida worker keeps more than a worker in almost every other state, because there is no state income tax to pay. On 75,000 dollars, a Florida resident keeps about 3,568 dollars more than an Illinois resident and about 2,484 dollars more than a North Carolina resident. Housing and insurance costs still vary, so a higher take-home does not always mean lower total living costs.
    Does Florida tax overtime or bonuses?
    Florida does not tax overtime, bonuses, or commissions at the state level, because it has no income tax at all. Federal rules still apply. Bonuses are usually subject to federal withholding as supplemental wages, and overtime is still counted as federal taxable income.
    What is the Florida payroll tax for employees?
    Florida employees pay only the federal payroll taxes, Social Security and Medicare, together known as FICA. Florida charges employers a reemployment tax that funds unemployment benefits, but that is paid by the employer, not deducted from your paycheck.

How Your Paycheck Is Calculated in 2026

Your gross salary is rarely what you take home. Between federal income tax, state income tax, FICA (Social Security and Medicare), retirement contributions, and benefit deductions, the typical American keeps only 65-78% of their gross pay. This calculator breaks down exactly where every dollar goes.

1. Federal Income Tax (Largest Single Deduction for Most Workers)

The U.S. uses a progressive tax system with seven brackets ranging from 10% to 37% in 2026. Your tax isn't a flat percentage, different portions of your income are taxed at different rates. For a single filer earning $65,000, the first $12,400 is taxed at 10%, the next $38,000 at 12%, and the remaining amount at 22%. Your effective rate ends up around 12-14%, even though your top bracket is 22%.

2. State Income Tax (Varies Wildly by Location)

State income tax can swing your take-home pay by thousands of dollars per year. Nine states have no income tax on wages: Alaska, Florida, Nevada, New Hampshire, South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, Washington, and Wyoming. On the opposite end, California's top rate is 13.3%, and Hawaii reaches 11%. Use our state comparison tool above to see exactly how a move would affect your paycheck.

3. FICA Taxes: Social Security + Medicare

Every W-2 employee pays 7.65% in FICA taxes, split as 6.2% for Social Security and 1.45% for Medicare. Social Security tax applies only to the first $184,500 of wages in 2026 (the "wage base"), so high earners get a partial break. Medicare has no cap, and earners above $200,000 (single) or $250,000 (married) pay an additional 0.9% Medicare surtax. If you work overtime, remember that FICA still applies to your overtime pay in full, even though a new federal deduction may reduce the income tax on part of it. See our Overtime Tax Calculator for how that works.

4. Pre-Tax Deductions Reduce Your Taxable Income

Pre-tax deductions like Traditional 401(k), HSA, FSA, and many health insurance premiums lower your federal and state taxable income. If you earn $65,000 in California and contribute 10% to your Traditional 401(k), you reduce your taxable income by $6,500, saving roughly $1,430 in federal tax and about $600 in California state tax. That's $2,030 in tax savings while building retirement wealth. Our 401(k) Calculator shows the long-term growth impact.

5. Why Identical Salaries Produce Different Paychecks

Two people earning $80,000 can take home vastly different amounts depending on: state of residence, filing status (single vs. married), pre-tax retirement contributions, HSA/FSA elections, health insurance premium share, and whether they have children or other tax credits. This calculator captures the major variables; your real paycheck may also be affected by local/city taxes (NYC charges 3-3.876%, San Francisco has a payroll tax), supplemental wages, and your W-4 withholding selections.

Next step: Once you know your real take-home, plug it into our Budget Planner to build a 50/30/20 budget, or our Savings Calculator to set goals around what you can actually save.

New for 2026: The No Tax on Overtime Deduction

Starting with the 2025 tax year, a new federal law lets many hourly workers deduct part of their overtime pay from their taxable income. It runs for tax years 2025 through 2028. The name is misleading, though: it is a deduction, not a tax-free exemption, and it only covers part of your overtime.

Only the FLSA half-premium qualifies, meaning the extra 0.5 times your regular rate on hours over 40 in a week, not your whole overtime check. If your regular rate is $20 and overtime pays $30 an hour, only the $10 premium is deductible. The deduction is capped at $12,500 for single filers and $25,000 for married filing jointly, and it phases out once your income passes $150,000 (single) or $300,000 (joint).

Important: This deduction lowers federal income tax only. Your overtime is still subject to the 7.65% in FICA taxes shown in your paycheck breakdown above, and many states still tax it in full.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much of my paycheck goes to taxes in 2026?

For a $65,000 salary, expect roughly 22-32% to go to combined federal, state, and FICA taxes depending on your state. No-income-tax states like Texas and Florida keep you in the 22-25% range; high-tax states like California and New York push you toward 30-32%.

What's the difference between gross pay and net pay?

Gross pay is your total salary before any deductions. Net pay (take-home pay) is what hits your bank account after federal income tax, state income tax, Social Security (6.2%), Medicare (1.45%), 401(k) contributions, health insurance, and other deductions are subtracted.

Which states have no income tax?

Nine states have no state income tax on wages: Alaska, Florida, Nevada, New Hampshire (no wage tax, only on interest/dividends), South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, Washington, and Wyoming. Moving to one of these states can increase your take-home pay by 3-13% depending on your salary and current state.

How do 401(k) contributions affect my take-home pay?

Traditional 401(k) contributions are pre-tax, they reduce your taxable income, lowering both federal and state tax. Roth 401(k) contributions are after-tax, they don't reduce your current tax but grow tax-free for retirement. Both reduce your current paycheck by the contribution amount, but Traditional gives you immediate tax savings.

What is the Social Security wage base for 2026?

The Social Security wage base for 2026 is $184,500 (confirmed by the SSA). You pay 6.2% Social Security tax only on income up to this limit. Income above this amount is exempt from Social Security tax but still subject to the 1.45% Medicare tax (plus an additional 0.9% above $200,000 for single filers).

How accurate is this paycheck calculator?

This calculator uses 2026 federal tax brackets (IRS Rev. Proc. 2025-32), the 2026 Social Security wage base of $184,500, current Medicare rates, and published state tax rates for all 50 states plus DC. It provides accurate estimates for most W-2 employees but does not account for local/city taxes (like NYC's 3-3.876%), supplemental wages, additional voluntary deductions, dependent tax credits, or special tax situations. Consult a tax professional for precise withholding.

Why is my actual paycheck different from this calculator?

Common reasons: your W-4 withholding settings, local/city income taxes, employer-specific benefit deductions, supplemental income (bonuses, RSUs), additional dependents claimed, or year-to-date adjustments. This calculator estimates standard withholding for most W-2 employees in a typical pay period.

Should I increase my 401(k) contribution to lower my taxes?

For most workers, contributing at least enough to get your full employer match is essential, it's effectively free money. Beyond that, increasing Traditional 401(k) contributions reduces current taxes and builds long-term wealth.

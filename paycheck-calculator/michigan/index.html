How the Michigan paycheck calculator figures your take-home pay

Michigan keeps the state math simple with one flat rate, then adds a twist that most states do not have: city income taxes. The Michigan paycheck calculator above starts with your gross salary, subtracts federal income tax and FICA, applies the 4.25 percent flat Michigan tax on your income after the personal exemption, and then adds a city income tax if you live or work in one of Michigan's 24 taxing cities. There are no state brackets, so someone earning 40,000 dollars and someone earning 400,000 dollars pay the same 4.25 percent state rate.

Worked example. A single filer earning 75,000 dollars in 2026 with no city tax owes about 7,670 dollars in federal income tax, 4,650 dollars in Social Security, and 1,088 dollars in Medicare. For Michigan, subtract the 5,900 dollar exemption and apply 4.25 percent, which is about 2,937 dollars. That leaves a take-home of roughly 58,655 dollars a year, or about 2,256 dollars in each biweekly paycheck. If that same worker lives in Detroit, the 2.4 percent city tax adds about 1,800 dollars, dropping take-home to around 56,855 dollars. The Michigan flat tax and the 2026 exemption

Michigan has used a flat income tax since 1967. The rate is 4.25 percent for 2026, confirmed by the Michigan Department of Treasury. It briefly fell to 4.05 percent for 2023 when a revenue trigger kicked in, but courts ruled that was a one-time event, so it returned to 4.25 percent for 2024 and later years. Instead of a standard deduction, Michigan gives a personal exemption of 5,900 dollars per person for 2026, up from 5,800 dollars in 2025. You claim one for yourself, one for a spouse on a joint return, and one for each dependent, so a family of four subtracts 23,600 dollars before the 4.25 percent rate applies.

City income taxes: the Michigan wrinkle

This is the part that catches people off guard, and it is exactly why a Michigan-specific calculator matters. Twenty-four Michigan cities levy their own income tax on top of the state rate. Detroit is the big one, charging residents 2.4 percent, the highest city income tax in the Midwest, and non-residents who work in the city 1.2 percent. Grand Rapids charges 1.5 percent for residents, and most other taxing cities, including Lansing, Flint, Saginaw, Pontiac, and Battle Creek, charge 1 percent for residents and 0.5 percent for non-residents.

The rule that trips people up is that city tax follows both where you live and where you work. A Troy resident who commutes to a Detroit office owes Detroit's 1.2 percent non-resident tax on those wages, even though Troy has no city tax of its own. This is why so many Detroit-area workers live in suburbs like Warren, Troy, or Dearborn: they pay the lower non-resident rate instead of the full 2.4 percent. On an 80,000 dollar salary, the gap between the resident and non-resident Detroit rate is about 960 dollars a year. Pick your city in the calculator above to see the difference.

Social Security and retirement income in Michigan

Michigan is kinder to retirees than its flat tax alone suggests. Social Security benefits are fully exempt from Michigan income tax for everyone, and so is military retirement pay. Pension and other retirement income is taxed on a tiered system based on your birth year, and the Lowering MI Costs law has been expanding those deductions through 2026, so younger retirees keep more than they used to. One caution: Michigan property taxes are near the national average and its city taxes stack up, so run any home purchase through our mortgage calculator.

How to increase your Michigan take-home pay

A pre-tax 401(k) is the strongest lever, and in Michigan it lowers both your federal tax and your 4.25 percent state tax, because Michigan starts from your federal income. It does not reduce city tax, which applies to gross wages, but it still helps everywhere else. An HSA works the same way. Frequently Asked Questions

What is the Michigan income tax rate for 2026?

Michigan has a flat income tax rate of 4.25 percent for 2026, confirmed by the Michigan Department of Treasury. Everyone pays the same rate no matter how much they earn. The rate briefly dropped to 4.05 percent for 2023 under a revenue trigger, then returned to 4.25 percent for 2024 and later years.

Does Detroit have a city income tax?

Yes. Detroit charges a 2.4 percent city income tax on residents and 1.2 percent on non-residents who work in the city, on top of the 4.25 percent state tax. Detroit's resident rate is the highest local income tax in the Midwest. In total, 24 Michigan cities levy a local income tax.

What is the Michigan personal exemption for 2026?

The 2026 Michigan personal exemption is 5,900 dollars per person, up from 5,800 dollars in 2025. You claim one for yourself, one for a spouse on a joint return, and one for each dependent. The exemption reduces your Michigan taxable income before the 4.25 percent rate applies. Michigan does not have a standard deduction.

How much is taken out of a Michigan paycheck?

For a single filer earning 75,000 dollars in 2026 with no city tax, about 7,670 dollars goes to federal income tax, 2,937 dollars to Michigan state tax, 4,650 dollars to Social Security, and 1,088 dollars to Medicare. That leaves a take-home of roughly 58,655 dollars a year. A Detroit resident would pay about 1,800 dollars more in city tax.

Does Michigan tax Social Security or retirement income?

Michigan does not tax Social Security benefits or military retirement pay for anyone. Pension and other retirement income is taxed on a tiered system based on your birth year, with the Lowering MI Costs law expanding deductions through 2026. Younger retirees should confirm their specific deduction with the Michigan Department of Treasury.

Which Michigan cities have a local income tax?

Twenty-four Michigan cities levy a local income tax, including Detroit, Grand Rapids, Lansing, Flint, Saginaw, Pontiac, Battle Creek, and Highland Park. Detroit charges 2.4 percent for residents, Grand Rapids charges 1.5 percent, and most other cities charge 1 percent for residents. Non-residents who work in a taxing city generally pay half the resident rate.

How does Michigan take-home pay compare to Illinois?

Michigan usually leaves you with a little more. Michigan's 4.25 percent flat rate is lower than Illinois's 4.95 percent, and Michigan's 5,900 dollar exemption is larger than Illinois's 2,925 dollars. On a 75,000 dollar salary, a Michigander outside a taxing city keeps roughly 600 dollars more than an Illinois worker. A Detroit resident, however, ends up close to or below the Illinois figure.

How do I calculate my Michigan take-home pay?

Start with your gross pay and subtract federal income tax based on the 2026 brackets, Social Security at 6.2 percent up to 184,500 dollars, and Medicare at 1.45 percent. For Michigan, subtract the 5,900 dollar personal exemption and multiply what remains by 4.25 percent. If you live or work in a taxing city like Detroit, add the city rate. Subtract any pre-tax 401(k). What is left is your take-home pay.

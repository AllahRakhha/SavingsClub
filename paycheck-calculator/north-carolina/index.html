How the North Carolina paycheck calculator figures your take-home pay

North Carolina keeps things simple with one flat rate and no local income tax. The North Carolina paycheck calculator above starts with your gross salary, subtracts federal income tax and FICA, and applies the 3.99 percent flat NC tax to your income after the state standard deduction. There are no state brackets and no city taxes anywhere in North Carolina, so a person earning 45,000 dollars and a person earning 450,000 dollars pay the same 3.99 percent state rate.

Worked example. A single filer earning 75,000 dollars in 2026 owes about 7,670 dollars in federal income tax, 4,650 dollars in Social Security, and 1,088 dollars in Medicare. For North Carolina, subtract the 12,750 dollar standard deduction and apply 3.99 percent, which is about 2,484 dollars in state tax. That leaves a take-home of roughly 59,109 dollars a year, or about 2,273 dollars in each biweekly paycheck. That is more than the same salary keeps in our Illinois or Michigan paycheck calculators, because North Carolina's rate is lower, though still less than a no-tax state like Texas.

North Carolina's falling flat tax, and why your take-home keeps rising

Here is the part that is genuinely good news for North Carolina workers. Since switching from graduated brackets to a flat tax in 2014, North Carolina has cut its rate almost every year: from 5.75 percent down through 4.75, 4.5, and 4.25 percent, to 3.99 percent for 2026. Under current law the rate can fall further to 3.49 percent in 2027 if the state hits its revenue targets, with more cuts possible after that. In plain terms, on the same salary your North Carolina state tax shrinks a little each year without you doing anything, so your take-home pay drifts upward. On a 75,000 dollar income, the drop from 4.25 to 3.99 percent alone is worth about 160 dollars more in your pocket this year versus last.

What comes out of a North Carolina paycheck

Federal income tax. Based on the 2026 IRS brackets and your filing status. See the full brackets in our federal income tax rate calculator.

North Carolina state tax. A flat 3.99 percent of your income after the standard deduction (12,750 dollars for single filers). North Carolina uses a standard deduction rather than a personal exemption, and there is no local or city income tax anywhere in the state.

Social Security. 6.2 percent of wages up to the 2026 wage base of 184,500 dollars.

Medicare. 1.45 percent of all wages, plus an extra 0.9 percent on wages above 200,000 dollars for single filers or 250,000 dollars for joint filers.

Social Security and retirement income in North Carolina

North Carolina fully exempts Social Security benefits from state tax, which helps retirees on fixed incomes. Private pension, 401(k), and IRA income is taxable at the 3.99 percent flat rate, though residents age 65 and older can deduct up to 4,000 dollars, and certain federal, state, and military pensions are exempt under the Bailey decision. If you are building toward retirement, model the growth in our retirement calculator or compare account types with our Roth IRA calculator. North Carolina also has below-average property taxes and no estate tax, which is part of why the Research Triangle and Charlotte keep drawing new residents.

How to increase your North Carolina take-home pay

A pre-tax 401(k) is the strongest lever, and in North Carolina it lowers both your federal tax and your 3.99 percent state tax, because North Carolina starts from your federal income. An HSA does the same if you have a high-deductible health plan. Add a 401(k) percentage in the calculator above to see the effect, then put the difference to work with our budget planner, an emergency fund, and a high-yield savings plan before anything else.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the North Carolina income tax rate for 2026?

North Carolina has a flat income tax rate of 3.99 percent for 2026, down from 4.25 percent in 2025. Everyone pays the same rate no matter how much they earn. North Carolina switched from graduated brackets to a flat tax in 2014 and has cut the rate almost every year since.

Is the North Carolina tax rate going down?

Yes. North Carolina's flat rate has fallen steadily from 5.75 percent in 2014 to 3.99 percent in 2026. Under current law it can drop to 3.49 percent in 2027 if state revenue targets are met, with further cuts possible. That means your North Carolina take-home pay tends to rise a little each year on the same salary.

What is the North Carolina standard deduction for 2026?

For 2026 the North Carolina standard deduction is 12,750 dollars for single filers and married filing separately, 25,500 dollars for married filing jointly, and 19,125 dollars for head of household. North Carolina uses this standard deduction instead of a personal exemption, which it eliminated in the 2014 reform.

Does North Carolina have a local or city income tax?

No. North Carolina does not allow any city or county to levy a local income tax on wages. Your paycheck is only subject to the 3.99 percent state tax plus federal tax and FICA, which keeps North Carolina simpler than states like Ohio or Michigan that have city income taxes.

How much is taken out of a North Carolina paycheck?

For a single filer earning 75,000 dollars in 2026, about 7,670 dollars goes to federal income tax, 2,484 dollars to North Carolina state tax, 4,650 dollars to Social Security, and 1,088 dollars to Medicare. That leaves a take-home of roughly 59,109 dollars a year, or about 2,273 dollars per biweekly paycheck.

Does North Carolina tax Social Security or retirement income?

North Carolina fully exempts Social Security benefits from state tax. Most private pension, 401(k), and IRA income is taxable at the 3.99 percent flat rate, though residents age 65 and older can deduct up to 4,000 dollars, and certain government pensions are exempt under the Bailey decision. Military retirement pay is also exempt for many filers.

How does North Carolina take-home pay compare to Illinois or Michigan?

North Carolina usually leaves you with the most of the three, because its 3.99 percent rate is lower than Michigan's 4.25 percent and Illinois's 4.95 percent. On a 75,000 dollar salary, a North Carolina worker keeps roughly 450 dollars more than a Michigander and about 1,085 dollars more than an Illinois worker.

How do I calculate my North Carolina take-home pay?

Start with your gross pay and subtract federal income tax based on the 2026 brackets, Social Security at 6.2 percent up to 184,500 dollars, and Medicare at 1.45 percent. For North Carolina, subtract the 12,750 dollar standard deduction and multiply what remains by 3.99 percent. Subtract any pre-tax 401(k). What is left is your take-home pay.

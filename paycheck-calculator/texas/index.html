How Texas paychecks work: no state income tax

Texas is one of only nine states with no state income tax, and its constitution actually bans a personal income tax without a statewide vote. That is the whole reason a Texas paycheck stretches further than the same salary in most other states. When you run the numbers in the Texas paycheck calculator above, you will notice there is no state tax line at all. The only taxes taken from your wages are federal income tax and FICA, which is Social Security and Medicare.

Worked example. Take a single filer earning 75,000 dollars in 2026. Federal income tax is about 7,670 dollars (after the 16,100 dollar standard deduction), Social Security takes 4,650 dollars, and Medicare takes 1,088 dollars. Texas state tax is zero. That leaves a take-home pay of about 61,593 dollars a year, or roughly 2,369 dollars in each biweekly paycheck. In a state like California, that same worker would lose around 3,000 dollars more to state income tax.

What actually comes out of a Texas paycheck

Only three things reduce a Texas paycheck, and none of them are state taxes:

Federal income tax. Based on the 2026 IRS brackets and your filing status, applied to your income after the standard deduction. See the full brackets in our federal income tax rate calculator.

Social Security. 6.2 percent of your wages up to the 2026 wage base of 184,500 dollars. Above that, no more Social Security tax is withheld for the year.

Medicare. 1.45 percent of all your wages with no cap, plus an extra 0.9 percent on wages above 200,000 dollars for single filers or 250,000 dollars for joint filers.

There is no Texas state income tax and no local city or county income tax anywhere in the state, which is unusual. States like Ohio and Pennsylvania stack municipal income taxes on top of the state rate; Texas has neither.

Texas has no income tax, but it is not tax-free

Here is the honest part most paycheck tools leave out. Texas makes up for the missing income tax with higher sales and property taxes. The state sales tax is 6.25 percent and reaches 8.25 percent with local add-ons, and Texas property taxes are among the highest in the country as a share of home value. So your paycheck is bigger, but if you own a home, a chunk of that advantage goes to your property tax bill. Renters and people who spend less tend to come out further ahead in Texas than homeowners do. Knowing this helps you plan realistically rather than assuming Texas is simply cheaper.

How to increase your Texas take-home pay

Since there is no state tax to reduce, your federal tax is the lever. Contributing to a pre-tax 401(k) lowers your federal taxable income, so more of every raise stays with you, and the calculator above lets you add a 401(k) percentage to see the effect. A pre-tax HSA works the same way if you have a high-deductible health plan. It is also worth checking your W-4 so you are not over-withholding and handing the IRS an interest-free loan all year. If you want to turn that larger Texas paycheck into real progress, route the difference into a plan with our budget planner and an emergency fund first, then long-term growth with a Roth IRA or retirement calculator.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does Texas have a state income tax?

No. Texas is one of nine states with no state income tax, and its constitution bans a personal income tax without a statewide vote. This means the only income taxes taken from a Texas paycheck are federal income tax and FICA (Social Security and Medicare). There is also no local city or county income tax in Texas.

How much is taken out of a Texas paycheck?

For a single filer earning 75,000 dollars in 2026, about 7,670 dollars goes to federal income tax, 4,650 dollars to Social Security, and 1,088 dollars to Medicare, for roughly 13,400 dollars total and a take-home of about 61,600 dollars a year. There is no Texas state tax, so nothing is withheld for the state.

What is the Texas income tax rate?

The Texas state income tax rate is zero percent. Texas does not tax wages, salaries, or other personal income. The state funds itself mainly through sales tax and property tax instead, which are higher than the national average.

How do I calculate my Texas take-home pay?

Start with your gross pay, then subtract federal income tax (based on the 2026 brackets and your filing status), Social Security at 6.2 percent up to 184,500 dollars of wages, and Medicare at 1.45 percent. Subtract any pre-tax deductions like a 401(k). What remains is your take-home pay. Because Texas has no state income tax, there is no state line to subtract.

Does Texas have local income taxes?

No. No city or county in Texas levies a local income tax on wages. Some states, like Ohio and Pennsylvania, have municipal income taxes on top of the state tax, but Texas has neither a state nor a local income tax.

How does Texas take-home pay compare to other states?

A Texan keeps more of each paycheck than a worker at the same salary in a state with income tax. On a 75,000 dollar salary, a Californian would owe roughly 3,000 dollars in state income tax that a Texan does not pay. However, Texas offsets this with higher property and sales taxes, so the total tax picture depends on your spending and whether you own a home.

What taxes do Texans pay instead of income tax?

Texas relies on sales tax and property tax. The state sales tax is 6.25 percent, and with local additions it can reach 8.25 percent. Property taxes are among the highest in the nation as a share of home value. So while your paycheck is larger in Texas, some of that advantage is spent on other taxes, especially if you own a home.

How can I increase my Texas take-home pay?

Because Texas has no state tax, your main lever is federal tax. Contributing to a pre-tax 401(k) or HSA lowers your federal taxable income, so more of your raise stays with you. Checking your W-4 so you are not over-withholding also helps. This calculator lets you add a 401(k) percentage to see the effect on both your tax and your take-home pay.

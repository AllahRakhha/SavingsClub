How Does the Roth IRA Calculator Work?

A Roth IRA calculator projects the future value of your Roth IRA based on your annual contributions, expected investment returns, and time until retirement. The math is straightforward compound interest, but the magic is in the tax treatment.

With a Roth IRA, you contribute after-tax dollars. The IRS does not give you a deduction for those contributions today. In return, every dollar of growth inside the account is permanently tax-free. When you withdraw the money in retirement (after age 59 1/2 and meeting the 5-year rule), you owe zero federal income tax on any of it. Not on your contributions, not on the decades of compound growth, not on dividends, not on capital gains. Zero.

The power of tax-free growth: Contributing the 2026 maximum of $7,000 per year for 30 years at 7 percent average annual returns produces approximately $661,000. About $451,000 of that is pure investment growth that comes out tax-free in retirement. In a taxable account, that same growth could cost $90,000 to $150,000 in capital gains taxes.

2026 Roth IRA Contribution Limits

The IRS sets the maximum amount you can contribute to a Roth IRA each year. These are the 2026 limits.

Standard Contribution Limit
$7,000 per year
Catch-Up Limit (age 50+)
$8,000 per year
Income Phase-Out (Single)
$146,000 to $161,000 MAGI
Income Phase-Out (MFJ)
$230,000 to $240,000 MAGI

How phase-out works: If your modified adjusted gross income (MAGI) falls within the phase-out range, your maximum contribution is reduced proportionally. For example, a single filer with $153,500 MAGI (halfway through the phase-out range) can contribute approximately half the maximum, or $3,500.

What if you exceed the income limit? You cannot contribute directly to a Roth IRA. But the backdoor Roth IRA strategy (described below) lets high earners legally access Roth IRA benefits through a Traditional IRA conversion.

Roth IRA vs Traditional IRA: Which Is Better?

This is the most asked question in retirement planning. The honest answer is: it depends on your current tax bracket vs your expected retirement tax bracket. Our side by side comparison above does the math for your specific numbers.

How Traditional IRA Works

Traditional IRA contributions are tax-deductible in the year you make them (assuming you meet income requirements and do not have a workplace retirement plan). Your money grows tax-deferred. When you withdraw in retirement, you pay ordinary income tax on the entire withdrawal, both your original contributions and decades of growth.

How Roth IRA Works

Roth IRA contributions are made with after-tax money. You get no deduction today. But growth is tax-free and qualified withdrawals in retirement are 100 percent tax-free. There are no required minimum distributions (RMDs) during your lifetime.

The Math: When Each One Wins

Roth IRA wins if:

  • You expect a higher tax bracket in retirement than today (likely for young workers with decades of earnings growth ahead)
  • You have many decades for tax-free growth to compound
  • You value tax certainty (today's rates locked in, regardless of future tax policy changes)
  • You want to avoid required minimum distributions in retirement
  • You want to leave tax-free money to heirs

Traditional IRA wins if:

  • You expect a significantly lower tax bracket in retirement
  • You are in your peak earning years (often age 50+) and want to lower current taxes
  • You need the tax deduction now to free up cash flow for other priorities
The honest rule of thumb: If you are under 40 and earning under $100,000, the Roth IRA is almost always the better choice. If you are over 50 earning $200,000+, the Traditional IRA's immediate tax deduction may serve you better.

Roth IRA Conversion Calculator: When It Makes Sense

A Roth conversion moves money from a Traditional IRA or 401(k) to a Roth IRA. You pay ordinary income taxes on the converted amount in the year of conversion. After conversion, all future growth is tax-free.

When Roth Conversion Makes Sense

  • Low-income years. Job loss, sabbatical, early retirement before Social Security begins. Your tax bracket may be temporarily low, making conversion cheap.
  • Before Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs). Traditional IRA RMDs start at age 73 (currently). Converting some Traditional money to Roth in your 60s reduces future forced taxable withdrawals.
  • Estate planning. Heirs inherit Roth IRAs tax-free. Conversion can shift the tax burden from your heirs to you, often at a lower combined rate.
  • Tax rate certainty. If you believe future tax rates will rise, paying tax now locks in today's rates.

When Roth Conversion Hurts

  • You expect a meaningfully lower tax bracket in retirement
  • You do not have outside money to pay the conversion tax (using IRA funds to pay tax forfeits future tax-free growth on that amount)
  • The conversion pushes you into a higher tax bracket this year

Use a Roth conversion tax calculator (this calculator does the basic math when you compare Roth vs Traditional above) to model your specific situation before converting.

What Is the Backdoor Roth IRA?

The backdoor Roth IRA is a legal strategy that lets high earners access Roth IRA benefits even when they exceed the direct contribution income limits.

How It Works

  1. Contribute up to $7,000 ($8,000 if 50+) to a Traditional IRA as a non-deductible contribution.
  2. Convert that Traditional IRA balance to a Roth IRA.
  3. The conversion is reported on IRS Form 8606.

Critical Warning: The Pro-Rata Rule

If you have any other Traditional IRA, SEP-IRA, or SIMPLE IRA balances with pre-tax money, the IRS pro-rata rule applies. The conversion is treated as proportionally taxable based on your total IRA balances. This can make backdoor Roth expensive or impractical.

The cleanest backdoor Roth setup: you have no other IRA balances. The strategy works best when started early in your career before accumulating Traditional IRA money.

Mega Backdoor Roth

If your 401(k) plan allows after-tax contributions and in-plan conversions or in-service distributions, you may be able to contribute up to $70,000 per year to a Roth via your 401(k) (2026 limit, including employer contributions). This is called the mega backdoor Roth IRA and requires specific 401(k) plan features.

Important: Backdoor Roth and mega backdoor Roth strategies have complex tax implications. Consult a qualified tax professional or CPA before executing.

Three Roth IRA Strategies Most People Miss

  1. Open a spousal Roth IRA for a non-working spouse

    If you file taxes jointly, the working spouse can contribute to a Roth IRA for the non-working spouse. This effectively doubles your couple's tax-free retirement savings space to $14,000 per year in 2026 ($16,000 if both spouses are 50+). Many couples miss this entirely.

  2. Contribute for the prior year until tax day

    You have until the April tax filing deadline to make a Roth IRA contribution for the previous year. If you forgot to contribute in 2025, you can still contribute up to $7,000 for tax year 2025 anytime before April 15, 2026.

  3. Withdraw contributions (not earnings) penalty-free at any age

    Unlike a 401(k) or Traditional IRA, you can withdraw your Roth IRA contributions (not earnings) at any time, for any reason, without taxes or penalties. This makes the Roth IRA an emergency backup option, though it is best left untouched to maximize tax-free growth.

Common Roth IRA Mistakes to Avoid

Not contributing because you cannot max out

Many people think they need to contribute the full $7,000 to make it worthwhile. False. Even $100 per month ($1,200 per year) for 30 years at 7 percent produces $122,000 tax-free. Start with what you can.

Picking the wrong investments inside the Roth IRA

A Roth IRA is an account type, not an investment. You still need to choose what to invest in. Most experts recommend low-cost index funds (S&P 500, total stock market, target-date funds) for long-term growth.

Forgetting about the 5-year rule

To withdraw earnings tax-free, you need two things: be age 59 1/2 or older AND have held the account for at least 5 years. The 5-year clock starts January 1 of the year of your first contribution.

Ignoring the Saver's Credit

If your income is low to moderate, the Saver's Credit (Retirement Savings Contributions Credit) gives you a tax credit of 10 to 50 percent of your Roth IRA contribution, up to $1,000 ($2,000 if married). Many young workers qualify and never claim it.

Cashing out when changing jobs

Unlike a 401(k), your Roth IRA is yours and does not change when you switch employers. Never withdraw it during job transitions. Let it keep growing tax-free.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much can you contribute to a Roth IRA in 2026?

The 2026 Roth IRA contribution limit is $7,000 if you are under age 50, or $8,000 if you are 50 or older (which includes a $1,000 catch-up contribution). These limits apply to total contributions across all Roth IRAs you own.

What is the Roth IRA income limit for 2026?

For 2026, single filers with modified adjusted gross income (MAGI) between $146,000 and $161,000 face a phase-out of contributions. Above $161,000, you cannot contribute directly to a Roth IRA. For married filing jointly, the phase-out range is $230,000 to $240,000.

Is a Roth IRA worth it?

For most Americans under 50, yes. Tax-free growth compounded over decades is extremely valuable. Contributing $7,000 per year for 30 years at 7 percent average returns produces approximately $661,000, with about $451,000 of that being pure investment growth that is withdrawn tax-free in retirement.

Roth IRA vs Traditional IRA: which is better?

Traditional IRA contributions are tax-deductible now but withdrawals are taxed in retirement. Roth IRA contributions are made with after-tax money but all qualified withdrawals are tax-free. If you expect to be in a higher tax bracket in retirement (or have many decades of growth ahead), Roth typically wins. If you expect a lower retirement tax bracket, Traditional may win.

Can you have a Roth IRA and a traditional IRA at the same time?

Yes. You can own both a Roth IRA and a Traditional IRA simultaneously. However, your combined annual contributions across both account types cannot exceed the IRS limit ($7,000 in 2026 if under 50, or $8,000 if 50 or older). The two account types can complement each other for tax diversification in retirement.

What is the backdoor Roth IRA strategy?

A backdoor Roth IRA is a legal strategy for high earners who exceed the Roth IRA income limits. You contribute non-deductible money to a Traditional IRA, then convert it to a Roth IRA. The conversion is taxable on any pre-tax amounts, so this works best if you have no other Traditional IRA balances. Consult a tax professional before executing.

Can I convert my 401k to a Roth IRA?

Yes. You can roll over a 401(k) to a Roth IRA, but the converted amount is treated as taxable income in the year of conversion. This can be smart if you are in a low tax bracket year (such as early retirement before Social Security and required minimum distributions begin). Use a Roth conversion tax calculator to estimate the tax cost before converting.

What is a spousal Roth IRA?

A spousal Roth IRA allows a working spouse to contribute to a Roth IRA for a non-working spouse. The couple must file taxes jointly and the working spouse must have enough earned income to cover both contributions. This effectively doubles a couple's tax-free retirement savings space to $14,000 per year in 2026 ($16,000 if both are 50+).

How Does a Roth IRA Calculator Work?

A Roth IRA calculator projects the future value of your Roth IRA based on annual contributions, expected investment returns, and time. The key advantage: all growth is tax-free if you follow the rules. The 2026 contribution limit is $7,000 ($8,000 if over 50).

Roth IRA vs. Traditional IRA

With a Traditional IRA, you contribute pre-tax dollars (reducing this year's taxes) but pay income tax on withdrawals in retirement. With a Roth IRA, you contribute after-tax dollars but withdrawals in retirement are completely tax-free. If you expect to be in a higher tax bracket in retirement, the Roth is usually better. If you expect lower taxes later, Traditional may win. Use our 401(k) Calculator to plan your overall retirement strategy.

Income Limits

For 2026, single filers with MAGI above $161,000 and married filing jointly above $240,000 face reduced or eliminated Roth IRA contribution eligibility. If you exceed these limits, consider a "backdoor Roth" — contribute to a Traditional IRA, then convert. Consult a tax professional for your specific situation.

The Power of Starting Early

Contributing $7,000 per year for 30 years at 7% average returns produces approximately $661,000 — but only $210,000 of that is your contributions. The remaining $451,000 is pure investment growth, all withdrawn tax-free. Starting just 5 years earlier could add over $200,000 to your final balance.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much can I put in a Roth IRA in 2026?

The 2026 limit is $7,000 if under 50, or $8,000 if 50 or older. Income limits apply: single filers above $161,000 MAGI face phaseouts.

Is a Roth IRA worth it?

For most Americans under 50, yes. Tax-free growth over decades is extremely powerful. Even if you can only contribute $100/month, the long-term tax savings are significant.
